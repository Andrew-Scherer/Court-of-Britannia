Core Principle: A Claimant is a player with a contested or unofficial claim to the throne. Unlike the official Heir, Claimants have no formal recognition ‚Äî their path to the Crown is through conflict, alliance, or seizing power during succession chaos.

What Is a Claimant?

A Claimant is any player who:

  • Believes they have a legitimate claim to the throne (merit, conquest, etc.)
  • Challenges the official Heir or other claimants for succession rights
  • Has NO official endorsement from the previous King
  • Must prove their claim through action ‚Äî military force, political maneuvering, or assassination

Claimants emerge especially during succession crises ‚Äî when a King dies without an Heir, or when the Heir is weak, contested, or blocked from coronation.

Why Claimants Emerge

Claimants arise in specific political conditions:

1. No Official Heir Exists

If the King dies without appointing an Heir, the Crown becomes a free-for-all. Any player with a plausible claim may declare themselves a Claimant and race to coronation.

2. The Heir Is Weak or Contested

If the official Heir is:

  • Unpopular or seen as illegitimate
  • Imprisoned or blocked by the Regent
  • Killed before they can be crowned

Then rival Claimants may challenge their right to the throne.

3. Merit-Based Claims

Some Claimants argue they deserve the throne based on:

  • Military prowess or conquest
  • Wealth, land ownership, or political influence
  • Service to the realm

5. Power Vacuums

When a King dies and both the Heir and Regent are absent, weak, or killed, the realm enters Interregnum ‚Äî a state of chaos where anyone can claim the throne.

How Claimants Challenge Succession

Claimants have several strategies to claim the Crown:

1. Race to Coronation

The simplest path: get to the coronation site first and crown yourself before the Heir or other rivals.

  • Risk: The Regent or Heir may block access to the coronation
  • Risk: Being crowned without legitimacy may trigger civil war
  • Reward: If successful, you become King ‚Äî even if contested
‚ö†Ô∏è Self-Crowning as a Claimant: If a Claimant crowns themselves, they technically become King ‚Äî but their legitimacy is weak. Loyalists to the Heir may refuse to recognize the new King and rebel.

2. Assassinate the Heir

If the official Heir is killed, their claim is voided. This clears the path for Claimants to:

  • Step up as the next strongest claim
  • Race to coronation without opposition
  • Negotiate with the Regent for support

3. Build Alliances

Claimants can gain support by:

  • Negotiating with the Regent: "Support my claim, and I'll keep you in power"
  • Recruiting noble houses: Gain vassal loyalty through promises of land, titles, or wealth
  • Public declarations: Announce their claim in Discord or in-game, rallying supporters

4. Military Uprising

If diplomatic paths fail, Claimants may:

  • Raise armies to challenge the Heir or Regent
  • Siege the coronation site to claim the Crown by force
  • Start a civil war to contest succession
Historical Parallel: This mirrors real-world succession conflicts like the Wars of the Roses, where rival claimants (Lancaster vs. York) fought for decades over who had the rightful claim to the English throne.

5. Delegitimize the Heir

Claimants may use political warfare to undermine the Heir's legitimacy:

  • "The previous King was a tyrant ‚Äî his appointed Heir has no right to rule"
  • "The Heir is not of true blood ‚Äî I am the rightful descendant"
  • "The Heir is weak and unfit ‚Äî I am the stronger candidate"

The Claimant's Gamble

Being a Claimant is extremely risky:

‚úÖ If You Win:

  • You become King/Queen and hold the Crown
  • Your claim is retroactively legitimized by victory
  • You gain control over the entire realm

‚ùå If You Lose:

  • You may be executed as a traitor or rebel
  • Your lands and titles may be seized
  • You lose all political standing and become an outlaw
  • Your house may be dishonored
‚ö° High Risk, High Reward: Claimants must weigh the potential for absolute power against the danger of total ruin. Declaring yourself a Claimant is a point of no return ‚Äî you either win the Crown or die trying.

Defending Against Claimants

If you are the Heir or Regent, you must deal with Claimants:

As the Heir:

  • Crown yourself quickly: The faster you complete coronation, the less time Claimants have to act
  • Secure alliances: Rally noble houses and the Regent (if possible) to your side
  • Bodyguards & protection: Claimants may assassinate you ‚Äî stay vigilant
  • Delegitimize Claimants: Publicly denounce them as traitors or pretenders

As the Regent:

  • Support the Heir: Crush Claimants militarily or politically
  • Negotiate with Claimants: Offer them lesser titles to stand down
  • Self-crown: If Claimants threaten stability, the Regent may seize the throne themselves
  • Execute Claimants: Use Regent authority to imprison or eliminate rivals

Types of Claims

Not all claims are equal. The strength of a claim affects how much political support a Claimant receives:

1. Strong Claims

  • Direct lineage: Child or sibling of the previous King
  • Historical dynasty: Descendant of a past ruling family
  • Popular support: Widely loved or respected by the realm

Strong claims gain more allies and legitimacy faster.

2. Weak Claims

  • Distant relatives: Cousins, distant lineage, or vague connections
  • Manufactured claims: "I bought this title" or "I won a contest"
  • No lineage: Pure merit or conquest-based claims

Weak claims require more force, politics, or cunning to succeed.

3. Fabricated Claims

  • False lineage: Claiming heritage that doesn't exist
  • Forged documents: Creating fake evidence of royal descent
  • Opportunistic claims: "I found a crown, so I'm King now"

Fabricated claims are the weakest and most likely to be rejected or crushed.

Multiple Claimants: The Chaos Scenario

When multiple Claimants emerge simultaneously, the realm descends into chaos:

  • Each Claimant races to coronation
  • Alliances form and break rapidly
  • Civil war may erupt as factions back different claimants
  • The Regent (if any) must choose which claimant to support ‚Äî or crush them all

War of Many Crowns

This scenario mirrors real-world succession wars where multiple factions vie for the throne. Think of the Wars of the Roses or the Sengoku period in Japan ‚Äî endless conflict until one victor emerges.

Historical Parallels

Claimant conflicts are rooted in real history:

  • Wars of the Roses (England, 1455-1487): Lancaster vs. York, two royal houses with competing claims
  • Henry VII (England, 1485): A weak claimant who won the throne through military victory at Bosworth Field
  • The Anarchy (England, 1135-1153): Matilda vs. Stephen, a 19-year civil war over who had the rightful claim
  • Sengoku Period (Japan, 1467-1615): Countless warlords claiming the right to rule, leading to decades of war
