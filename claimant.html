‚öîÔ∏è Claimants

Contested Blood ‚Äî Power Through Force

Core Principle: A Claimant is a player with a contested or unofficial claim to the throne. Unlike the official Heir, Claimants have no formal recognition ‚Äî their path to the Crown is through conflict, alliance, or seizing power during succession chaos.

What Is a Claimant?

A Claimant is any player who:

  • Believes they have a legitimate claim to the throne (merit, conquest, etc.)
  • Challenges the official Heir or other claimants for succession rights
  • Has NO official endorsement from the previous King
  • Must prove their claim through action ‚Äî military force, political maneuvering, or assassination

Claimants emerge especially during succession crises ‚Äî when a King dies without an Heir, or when the Heir is weak, contested, or blocked from coronation.

Claimant vs. Heir vs. Regent

When a King dies, three types of power contenders may emerge:

Aspect Heir Presumptive Regent Claimant
Official Recognition ‚úÖ Yes (appointed by King) ‚úÖ Yes (appointed by King) ‚ùå No (self-declared)
Claim to Throne ‚úÖ Strongest (official designation) ‚ùå None (unless self-declared) ‚ö†Ô∏è Contested (heritage/merit/force)
King Powers ‚ùå None ‚úÖ Full delegated authority ‚ùå None (unless they seize control)
Path to Crown Coronation (official) Self-crown (usurpation) Conflict, alliance, or self-crown
Legitimacy High (King's endorsement) Low (no claim to throne) Variable (depends on support)

üìå Key Difference: The Heir has official legitimacy but no power. The Regent has power but no claim. The Claimant has neither ‚Äî they must take both through force, politics, or cunning.

See What Happens When the King Dies for the full three-way dynamic.

Why Claimants Emerge

Claimants arise in specific political conditions:

1. No Official Heir Exists

If the King dies without appointing an Heir, the Crown becomes a free-for-all. Any player with a plausible claim may declare themselves a Claimant and race to coronation.

2. The Heir Is Weak or Contested

If the official Heir is:

  • Unpopular or seen as illegitimate
  • Imprisoned or blocked by the Regent
  • Killed before they can be crowned

Then rival Claimants may challenge their right to the throne.

3. Merit-Based Claims

Some Claimants argue they deserve the throne based on:

  • Military prowess or conquest
  • Wealth, land ownership, or political influence
  • Service to the realm

5. Power Vacuums

When a King dies and both the Heir and Regent are absent, weak, or killed, the realm enters Interregnum ‚Äî a state of chaos where anyone can claim the throne.

Can a Claimant Hold Official Court Positions?

üî• Critical Rule: Role-Stacking is Allowed

Yes. A player can be a member of a Guild AND a Faction (which is a layer on top of guilds) AND also hold an official role in Court and Crown.

Important: Factions are NOT separate from guilds ‚Äî they are voluntary alignment layers that guilds join. You're a guild member first, and your guild may choose to align with a faction.

This means:

  • A King can ALSO lead a guild that is aligned with a faction
  • A Marshal can ALSO lead a military guild that flies a faction banner
  • A Magistrate can ALSO head a guild that is part of a religious faction
  • A Claimant can ALSO hold Marshal, Magistrate, or other court offices while leading a guild aligned with a faction

The only restriction: You cannot hold multiple major court offices simultaneously (e.g., King + Marshal). But Guild leadership and Faction alignment do NOT count as court offices ‚Äî they're separate power structures entirely.

Why This Matters: Title Stacking as Power Fusion

In most pre-modern societies, Claimants rarely stood alone. They were backed by ‚Äî and often led ‚Äî the institutions that gave them power:

  • Religious institutions (churches, cults, sacred orders)
  • Noble factions (dynastic alliances, great houses)
  • Military orders (knight orders, warrior brotherhoods)
  • Economic guilds (merchant leagues, craft unions)

Holding a church office, faction leadership, or sacred role alongside a court position:

  • Boosted legitimacy ‚Äî "I am not just King, I am also God's chosen representative"
  • Neutralized rivals ‚Äî Challenging you means challenging multiple power structures
  • Framed rebellion as heresy or treason ‚Äî Opposition becomes morally wrong, not just politically inconvenient
  • Created dual loyalty networks ‚Äî Your followers answer to you in multiple capacities
‚ö° This Changes Everything: Factions and Guilds can not just vie for control of noble positions ‚Äî they can directly claim the Crown itself while maintaining their organizational power base. This isn't the exception. This is how power usually worked.

Historical Parallels: Political + Religious/Factional Fusion

üìú The Caliphate Model

Caliphs in Islamic history held both:

  • Political authority as rulers and military commanders
  • Religious authority as successors to the Prophet and leaders of the faithful

Challenging a Caliph meant not just political rebellion but religious apostasy. This dual authority made them extraordinarily difficult to depose.

‚õ™ Prince-Bishops and Cardinal-Kings

Medieval Europe saw numerous examples of religious leaders who also held secular power:

  • Prince-Bishops ‚Äî Bishops who ruled territories as feudal lords
  • Cardinal Richelieu ‚Äî Chief Minister of France and a Cardinal of the Catholic Church
  • Cardinal Wolsey ‚Äî Lord Chancellor of England and a Cardinal
  • Archbishop-Electors ‚Äî Religious leaders who elected Holy Roman Emperors

These figures wielded both ecclesiastical and temporal power, making them nearly untouchable.

üëë The Holy Roman Emperor

The Emperor was:

  • Secular ruler of the empire
  • Elected by Prince-Electors (including Archbishop-Electors)
  • Crowned by the Pope, giving religious legitimacy

This fusion of political power + religious endorsement + electoral backing created a triple-layer legitimacy that was extraordinarily difficult to challenge.

‚öîÔ∏è Military Orders with Political Power

Knights Templar, Knights Hospitaller, Teutonic Knights:

  • Religious military orders with their own territories
  • Answered to the Pope, not secular kings
  • Held castles, collected taxes, fielded armies
  • Leaders were both religious authorities and military commanders

The Grand Master of the Teutonic Order eventually became a ruling prince of Prussia ‚Äî merging religious, military, and political authority into one office.

üèõÔ∏è Merchant-Princes and Guild-Rulers

Venice, Florence, and other Italian city-states:

  • Doges and ruling councils were often heads of powerful merchant guilds
  • The Medici family were bankers AND rulers of Florence
  • Economic power and political power were openly fused

Separating "guild leader" from "political ruler" would have been nonsensical in these contexts ‚Äî they were the same people.

What This Means for Britannia

In Kingdoms of Britannia, this principle creates dramatic new possibilities:

A King who is also the High Priest of a religious faction can frame dissent as heresy.

A Marshal who also leads a mercenary guild controls both official guards and private armies.

A High Emissary who also heads a military guild wields a dangerous combination of both diplomacy and might.

A Claimant who leads both a faction AND holds a high ranking noble position has legitimacy (official position) + external backing (faction). This makes for a very politically strong combination to make a play for the throne.

This creates layered power structures where removing someone from one position doesn't eliminate their influence. It also means:

  • Coups can come from inside ‚Äî Your Marshal might already be a faction leader planning to seize the throne
  • Loyalty is complex ‚Äî Does a guard follow the Marshal's orders as Marshal, or as leader of their shared guild?
  • Exile doesn't work automatically ‚Äî Stripping someone of their court position doesn't also remove their guild/faction power base
  • Assassination is riskier ‚Äî Killing a King who also leads a religious faction creates martyrdom + succession crisis + religious war
‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Implication: The line between "legitimate government" and "faction control" becomes blurred. A faction doesn't need to "infiltrate" the government ‚Äî its leader can openly hold both positions. This is working as intended. It mirrors how real power operated throughout history.

Does This Lead to Corruption and Nepotism?

ü§ù Yes. Obviously.

Nepotism and corruption are inevitable consequences of role-stacking. A King who also leads a merchant guild will appoint guild members to court positions. A Marshal who commands a military order will staff the guards with their faction brothers. A Magistrate who heads a religious order will favor the faithful in legal rulings.

This is not a bug. This is human nature.

Corruption Is Historically Accurate

Every institution across human history has been affected by nepotism, favoritism, and corruption:

  • Medieval Catholic Church ‚Äî Positions sold for money (simony), families controlling bishoprics for generations
  • Royal Courts ‚Äî Favorites appointed over competent candidates, family networks dominating offices
  • Guilds ‚Äî Master craftsmen ensuring their sons inherited positions, excluding outsiders
  • Military Orders ‚Äî Knights favoring their brothers-in-arms for promotions and honors
  • Political Dynasties ‚Äî Entire families controlling government for centuries (Borgias, Medicis, etc.)

Attempting to create a "corruption-free" system would be historically nonsensical and dramatically sterile.

Why Game Mechanics Cannot (and Should Not) Prevent Corruption

There is no mechanical way to prevent a King from appointing their friends, guildmates, or faction brothers to positions. Any attempt to do so would:

  • Remove player agency ‚Äî "You can't appoint this person" kills political freedom
  • Create arbitrary restrictions ‚Äî Who decides what is "corrupt" vs "legitimate"?
  • Eliminate interesting conflicts ‚Äî Accusations of nepotism are dramatic content
  • Feel artificial and game-y ‚Äî Real power doesn't work that way
The point is not to prevent corruption. The point is to make corruption visible, consequential, and contestable.

How Permadeath Prevents Stagnant Corruption

The key difference between Britannia and stagnant power structures is permadeath:

  • Corrupt rulers die ‚Äî A King who fills court with incompetent loyalists creates vulnerability to assassination or coup
  • Networks fracture ‚Äî When the guild leader controlling multiple positions dies, their network loses cohesion
  • New players can rise ‚Äî Each death creates openings for outsiders to break into power
  • Grudges accumulate ‚Äî Extended nepotism creates enemies who remember when power shifts
  • Incompetence has consequences ‚Äî Appointing your brother who can't fight makes your guards weak in actual combat

Corruption becomes temporary rather than permanent because characters die and power structures collapse.

Corruption as Content, Not a Problem

In Britannia, accusations of corruption drive stories:

Dramatic Scenarios Corruption Creates

  • Public Accusation: "The King has filled the court with his guild cronies! This is tyranny!"
  • Reformer Movements: A challenger promises to "clean house" and appoint based on merit
  • Internal Rebellion: Excluded nobles band together to overthrow the corrupt regime
  • Assassination Justification: "I killed the Marshal because he was a corrupt guild puppet"
  • Civil War: Factions fight over whether the current power structure is legitimate

These are exactly the kinds of conflicts we want. Corruption is the spark, not the failure state.

What Players Can Do About Corruption

While mechanics don't prevent corruption, players can respond to it:

Response Method
Public Condemnation Call out nepotism publicly, damage the ruler's legitimacy
Competitive Merit Demonstrate superior competence to shame corrupt appointees
Alliance Building Unite excluded factions against the corrupt regime
Assassination Remove corrupt officials through permadeath
Coup/Revolution Overthrow the entire power structure and install new leadership
Alternative Institutions Form rival guilds/factions with different values
‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Design Philosophy: Corruption is not a design flaw to be patched out. It is working historical gameplay that creates dramatic tension, moral choices, and opportunities for conflict. The question is never "How do we prevent corruption?" but rather "What will you do about it?"

Resurrection Politics: When the Same Player Returns to Power

üîÑ The Edge Case

A King who leads a powerful faction dies (permadeath). His Regent, Heir, guards, and court are all faction members. His player respawns as a new character. At the coronation ceremony, the faction crowns the new character ‚Äî putting the same player back on the throne.

Is this allowed? Yes.

Is this a problem? No ‚Äî if players respond to it.

Why This Is Philosophically Consistent

This scenario is not circumventing permadeath. Here's why:

  • The character died permanently ‚Äî No resurrection, no respawn with same character sheet
  • The player has no automatic claim ‚Äî The new character starts with nothing
  • The faction chose to crown them ‚Äî This is an institutional decision, not a mechanical bypass
  • This tests institutional resilience ‚Äî If the faction maintained total control through a succession crisis, that proves organizational power
From Philosophy: "Death tests succession, loyalty, economic upkeep, and institutional resilience. A guild that survives its founder's death proves legitimacy. A faction that collapses when its leader dies was never an organization ‚Äî it was a personality cult."

If a faction can maintain complete control after their leader's death and choose to crown the same player's new character, that demonstrates:

  • The faction is real, not a personality cult
  • Institutional structures survived the death
  • Loyalty transfers to the organization, not just the individual
  • The player earned that loyalty through past actions

This is dynastic/factional control ‚Äî exactly the kind of power consolidation that should be possible (and challengeable).

The Critical Restriction

The philosophy establishes one hard rule: "No alts holding power simultaneously."

This means:

  • ‚úÖ Sequential ‚Äî Player 1 dies, then Player 1's new character is crowned later = ALLOWED
  • ‚ùå Simultaneous ‚Äî Player 1 is King while also controlling Marshal with an alt = FORBIDDEN

As long as there's true death and succession between characters, the system is working as intended.

Why This Is Politically Vulnerable

While mechanically allowed, this scenario creates massive political exposure:

Vulnerabilities of "Resurrection Rulership"

  • Delegitimization: "He's just a puppet! The faction controls everything, not the King!"
  • Target Identification: Everyone knows WHO to assassinate to break faction control
  • Corruption Narrative: This becomes perfect ammunition for reformers and rival factions
  • Weak Legitimacy: "He died once and they crowned him again? He'll die again soon."
  • Chronicle Tracking: Public records show "Third coronation of [Player Name]" ‚Äî pattern becomes obvious
  • Internal Fracture Risk: Faction members may question why they're serving the same person again

Player Responses to Resurrection Politics

This scenario is designed to provoke conflict. Here's how players can respond:

Response Type Method Effect
Public Challenge Declare the King a faction puppet, demand reform Damages legitimacy, rallies opposition
Targeted Assassination Kill the "resurrected" King again to prove pattern If successful, undermines faction's ability to protect their chosen leader
Counter-Faction Form rival faction with different values ("Merit over dynasties") Creates institutional competition
Internal Subversion Recruit disgruntled faction members tired of serving same person Fractures the power base from within
Chronicle Weaponization Point to public death records showing repeated resurrections Makes pattern undeniable, fuels reform movements
Reformer Campaign Run for King as a claimant promising "real change, not puppet kings" Offers alternative legitimacy narrative

Historical Parallels: Dynastic Resilience

This scenario mirrors real historical patterns:

The Medici Returns (15th-16th Century)

The Medici family was expelled from Florence twice (1494 and 1527), yet returned to power both times because their institutional network (banking, church connections, noble alliances) remained intact. Individual Medicis died or were exiled, but the family/faction reconsolidated power.

Opponents called them puppet masters and tyrants ‚Äî yet they kept winning because their organizational control was real.

The Restoration Monarchies

English monarchy was abolished in 1649 (Charles I beheaded). In 1660, his son Charles II was restored to the throne. Same dynasty, different person. Why? The institutional support (nobility, church, military) never fully shifted. The system wanted monarchy back.

This wasn't "resurrection" ‚Äî it was institutional resilience.

Soviet Nomenklatura

Individual Soviet leaders died (Stalin 1953, Khrushchev ousted 1964, Brezhnev died 1982), but the same institutional network (party members, KGB, military officers) controlled succession. Different faces, same power structure. The organization outlived individuals.

In all cases: Individuals died. Institutions survived. Power persisted.

When Does This Become a Problem?

This scenario only becomes problematic if players stop responding to it. If:

  • No one assassinates the "resurrected" King
  • No one forms rival factions
  • No one challenges faction dominance
  • No one weaponizes the Chronicle against them

Then the faction earned that dominance through player inaction. The system didn't fail ‚Äî the opposition did.

‚ö†Ô∏è Design Philosophy: If a faction maintains such total control that they can repeatedly crown the same player's characters, they have won the political game ‚Äî until someone successfully challenges them. This is not a bug. This is the intended endgame of factional power, and should be respected as such until opponents organize effectively.

The Chronicle Solution

The public Chronicle (death records, coronation records, succession records) makes this pattern impossible to hide:

  • "King [Character Name A] died [Date], cause: [assassination/combat/execution]"
  • "Coronation: [Character Name B] crowned [Date] ‚Äî [Player Name: same as deceased King]"
  • "Coronation: [Character Name C] crowned [Date] ‚Äî [Player Name: third coronation]"

This ensures:

  • Transparency: Everyone knows the pattern
  • Accountability: The faction can't deny it
  • Ammunition: Opposition has documented proof for propaganda
  • Drama: The pattern becomes a narrative hook for conflict

Summary: Resurrection Politics as Working Gameplay

Same player, different character, repeated power = Faction dynastic control

This is:
‚úÖ Mechanically allowed (sequential, not simultaneous)
‚úÖ Philosophically consistent (tests institutional resilience)
‚úÖ Historically accurate (dynasties and factions persisted through individual deaths)
‚úÖ Politically vulnerable (creates legitimate challenge opportunities)
‚úÖ Dramatically rich (provokes reform movements, assassinations, coups)

The question is not "Should this be prevented?"
The question is "Why haven't you stopped them yet?"

Building Power Outside Britain

Claimants usually (though not exclusively) build their base of power outside of Britain city proper. This is exactly what makes them so dangerous ‚Äî they're able to organize in secret, right under the King's nose, and eventually make an attempt on the throne.

This is why claimant strategy is deeply relevant to the Guild System and Faction System. Claimants build their power base through guilds (the sovereign organizational structure), and their guild may also align with a faction (a voluntary identity layer on top of guilds). Eventually they attempt to take (or fail to take) the throne.

But here's the twist: They don't always stay outside. A successful Claimant might first secure a court position (Marshal, Magistrate, Regent) while simultaneously leading a guild that is aligned with a faction. This gives them both internal legitimacy and external power ‚Äî the perfect combination for a successful coup.

Why Outside Bases Were Common

1. Exile = Safety + Freedom to Organize

If you stayed inside the contested territory, you risked arrest, assassination, or being politically boxed in. Outside Britain's walls, you could:

  • Raise troops
  • Gather money
  • Build alliances
  • Plan without constant surveillance

Exile wasn't weakness ‚Äî it was often a strategic reset.

2. Foreign Legitimacy and Resources

External powers (factions, guilds, outlying settlements) could provide:

  • Armies
  • Ships
  • Funding
  • Diplomatic recognition

That recognition alone could flip internal loyalties once the claimant returned.

3. Distance Creates Mystique

Being absent sometimes helped. Claimants could:

  • Avoid blame for current suffering
  • Be idealized as a "rightful ruler"
  • Let dissatisfaction inside the territory ripen on its own

Historical Examples

Henry Tudor (Henry VII, England)

  • Built his power base in Brittany and France
  • Returned with foreign troops
  • Won at Bosworth (1485) and took the crown

William the Conqueror

  • Duke of Normandy, not England
  • Used continental power, ships, and alliances
  • Invaded and claimed England in 1066

Napoleon Bonaparte

  • His early power base was the army, not Parisian politics
  • Foreign campaigns (Italy, Egypt) created prestige
  • Returned as a "savior" figure

Lenin

  • Built revolutionary networks largely in exile
  • Returned when the internal situation collapsed enough to allow a takeover
‚ö†Ô∏è The Pattern: Successful claimants often built power where the current regime couldn't reach them, then returned when conditions favored their claim. Factions and guilds provide exactly this opportunity ‚Äî external bases of power that can nurture a rival claim until the moment is right.

How Claimants Challenge Succession

Claimants have several strategies to claim the Crown:

1. Race to Coronation

The simplest path: get to the coronation site first and crown yourself before the Heir or other rivals.

  • Risk: The Regent or Heir may block access to the coronation
  • Risk: Being crowned without legitimacy may trigger civil war
  • Reward: If successful, you become King ‚Äî even if contested
‚ö†Ô∏è Self-Crowning as a Claimant: If a Claimant crowns themselves, they technically become King ‚Äî but their legitimacy is weak. Loyalists to the Heir may refuse to recognize the new King and rebel.

2. Assassinate the Heir

If the official Heir is killed, their claim is voided. This clears the path for Claimants to:

  • Step up as the next strongest claim
  • Race to coronation without opposition
  • Negotiate with the Regent for support

3. Build Alliances

Claimants can gain support by:

  • Negotiating with the Regent: "Support my claim, and I'll keep you in power"
  • Recruiting noble houses: Gain vassal loyalty through promises of land, titles, or wealth
  • Public declarations: Announce their claim in Discord or in-game, rallying supporters

4. Military Uprising

If diplomatic paths fail, Claimants may:

  • Raise armies to challenge the Heir or Regent
  • Siege the coronation site to claim the Crown by force
  • Start a civil war to contest succession
Historical Parallel: This mirrors real-world succession conflicts like the Wars of the Roses, where rival claimants (Lancaster vs. York) fought for decades over who had the rightful claim to the English throne.

5. Delegitimize the Heir

Claimants may use political warfare to undermine the Heir's legitimacy:

  • "The previous King was a tyrant ‚Äî his appointed Heir has no right to rule"
  • "The Heir is not of true blood ‚Äî I am the rightful descendant"
  • "The Heir is weak and unfit ‚Äî I am the stronger candidate"

The Claimant's Gamble

Being a Claimant is extremely risky:

‚úÖ If You Win:

  • You become King/Queen and hold the Crown
  • Your claim is retroactively legitimized by victory
  • You gain control over the entire realm

‚ùå If You Lose:

  • You may be executed as a traitor or rebel
  • Your lands and titles may be seized
  • You lose all political standing and become an outlaw
  • Your house may be dishonored
‚ö° High Risk, High Reward: Claimants must weigh the potential for absolute power against the danger of total ruin. Declaring yourself a Claimant is a point of no return ‚Äî you either win the Crown or die trying.

Defending Against Claimants

If you are the Heir or Regent, you must deal with Claimants:

As the Heir:

  • Crown yourself quickly: The faster you complete coronation, the less time Claimants have to act
  • Secure alliances: Rally noble houses and the Regent (if possible) to your side
  • Bodyguards & protection: Claimants may assassinate you ‚Äî stay vigilant
  • Delegitimize Claimants: Publicly denounce them as traitors or pretenders

As the Regent:

  • Support the Heir: Crush Claimants militarily or politically
  • Negotiate with Claimants: Offer them lesser titles to stand down
  • Self-crown: If Claimants threaten stability, the Regent may seize the throne themselves
  • Execute Claimants: Use Regent authority to imprison or eliminate rivals

Types of Claims

Not all claims are equal. The strength of a claim affects how much political support a Claimant receives:

1. Strong Claims

  • Direct lineage: Child or sibling of the previous King
  • Historical dynasty: Descendant of a past ruling family
  • Popular support: Widely loved or respected by the realm

Strong claims gain more allies and legitimacy faster.

2. Weak Claims

  • Distant relatives: Cousins, distant lineage, or vague connections
  • Manufactured claims: "I bought this title" or "I won a contest"
  • No lineage: Pure merit or conquest-based claims

Weak claims require more force, politics, or cunning to succeed.

3. Fabricated Claims

  • False lineage: Claiming heritage that doesn't exist
  • Forged documents: Creating fake evidence of royal descent
  • Opportunistic claims: "I found a crown, so I'm King now"

Fabricated claims are the weakest and most likely to be rejected or crushed.

Multiple Claimants: The Chaos Scenario

When multiple Claimants emerge simultaneously, the realm descends into chaos:

  • Each Claimant races to coronation
  • Alliances form and break rapidly
  • Civil war may erupt as factions back different claimants
  • The Regent (if any) must choose which claimant to support ‚Äî or crush them all

War of Many Crowns

This scenario mirrors real-world succession wars where multiple factions vie for the throne. Think of the Wars of the Roses or the Sengoku period in Japan ‚Äî endless conflict until one victor emerges.

Historical Parallels

Claimant conflicts are rooted in real history:

  • Wars of the Roses (England, 1455-1487): Lancaster vs. York, two royal houses with competing claims
  • Henry VII (England, 1485): A weak claimant who won the throne through military victory at Bosworth Field
  • The Anarchy (England, 1135-1153): Matilda vs. Stephen, a 19-year civil war over who had the rightful claim
  • Sengoku Period (Japan, 1467-1615): Countless warlords claiming the right to rule, leading to decades of war
