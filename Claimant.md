## What Is a Claimant?

A **Claimant** is any player who:


- Believes they have a **legitimate claim** to the throne (merit, conquest, etc.)
- Challenges the official Heir or other claimants for succession rights
- Has **NO official endorsement** from the previous King
- Must **prove their claim through action** â€” military force, political maneuvering, or assassination

Claimants emerge especially during **succession crises** â€” when a King dies without an Heir, or when the Heir is weak, contested, or blocked from coronation.

## Why Claimants Emerge

Claimants arise in specific political conditions:


### 1. No Official Heir Exists

If the King dies **without appointing an Heir**, the Crown becomes a free-for-all. Any player with a plausible claim may declare themselves a Claimant and race to coronation.


### 2. The Heir Is Weak or Contested

If the official Heir is:


- Unpopular or seen as illegitimate
- Imprisoned or blocked by the Regent
- Killed before they can be crowned

Then rival Claimants may challenge their right to the throne.


### 3. Merit-Based Claims

Some Claimants argue they **deserve** the throne based on:


- Military prowess or conquest
- Wealth, land ownership, or political influence
- Service to the realm


### 5. Power Vacuums

When a King dies and both the Heir and Regent are absent, weak, or killed, the realm enters **Interregnum** â€” a state of chaos where anyone can claim the throne.

## Does This Lead to Corruption and Nepotism?


> **Note**
> **ðŸ¤ Yes. Obviously.**
>
> **Nepotism and corruption are inevitable consequences of role-stacking.** A King who also leads a merchant guild will appoint guild members to court positions. A Marshal who commands a military order will staff the guards with their faction brothers. A Magistrate who heads a religious order will favor the faithful in legal rulings.
> **This is not a bug. This is human nature.**


### Corruption Is Historically Accurate

Every institution across human history has been affected by nepotism, favoritism, and corruption:


- **Medieval Catholic Church** â€” Positions sold for money (simony), families controlling bishoprics for generations
- **Royal Courts** â€” Favorites appointed over competent candidates, family networks dominating offices
- **Guilds** â€” Master craftsmen ensuring their sons inherited positions, excluding outsiders
- **Military Orders** â€” Knights favoring their brothers-in-arms for promotions and honors
- **Political Dynasties** â€” Entire families controlling government for centuries (Borgias, Medicis, etc.)

Attempting to create a "corruption-free" system would be **historically nonsensical** and dramatically sterile.


### Why Game Mechanics Cannot (and Should Not) Prevent Corruption

There is no mechanical way to prevent a King from appointing their friends, guildmates, or faction brothers to positions. Any attempt to do so would:


- **Remove player agency** â€” "You can't appoint this person" kills political freedom
- **Create arbitrary restrictions** â€” Who decides what is "corrupt" vs "legitimate"?
- **Eliminate interesting conflicts** â€” Accusations of nepotism are dramatic content
- **Feel artificial and game-y** â€” Real power doesn't work that way


> The point is not to prevent corruption.The point is to make corruptionvisible, consequential, and contestable.


### How Permadeath Prevents Stagnant Corruption

The key difference between Britannia and stagnant power structures is **permadeath**:


- **Corrupt rulers die** â€” A King who fills court with incompetent loyalists creates vulnerability to assassination or coup
- **Networks fracture** â€” When the guild leader controlling multiple positions dies, their network loses cohesion
- **New players can rise** â€” Each death creates openings for outsiders to break into power
- **Grudges accumulate** â€” Extended nepotism creates enemies who remember when power shifts
- **Incompetence has consequences** â€” Appointing your brother who can't fight makes your guards weak in actual combat

Corruption becomes **temporary** rather than permanent because characters die and power structures collapse.


### Corruption as Content, Not a Problem

In Britannia, accusations of corruption **drive stories**:


> **Note**
> **Dramatic Scenarios Corruption Creates**
>
> - **Public Accusation:** "The King has filled the court with his guild cronies! This is tyranny!"
> - **Reformer Movements:** A challenger promises to "clean house" and appoint based on merit
> - **Internal Rebellion:** Excluded nobles band together to overthrow the corrupt regime
> - **Assassination Justification:** "I killed the Marshal because he was a corrupt guild puppet"
> - **Civil War:** Factions fight over whether the current power structure is legitimate

These are **exactly the kinds of conflicts we want**. Corruption is the spark, not the failure state.

## Resurrection Politics: When the Same Player Returns to Power


> **Note**
> **ðŸ”„ The Edge Case**
>
> A King who leads a powerful faction dies (permadeath). His Regent, Heir, guards, and court are all faction members. His **player** respawns as a new character. At the coronation ceremony, the faction crowns **the new character** â€” putting the same **player** back on the throne.
> **Is this allowed?** Yes.
> **Is this a problem?** No â€” if players respond to it.


### Why This Is Philosophically Consistent

This scenario is **not** circumventing permadeath. Here's why:


- **The character died permanently** â€” No resurrection, no respawn with same character sheet
- **The player has no automatic claim** â€” The new character starts with nothing
- **The faction chose to crown them** â€” This is an institutional decision, not a mechanical bypass
- **This tests institutional resilience** â€” If the faction maintained total control through a succession crisis, that proves organizational power


> From Philosophy:"Death tests succession, loyalty, economic upkeep, and institutional resilience. A guild that survives its founder's death proves legitimacy. A faction that collapses when its leader dies was never an organization â€” it was a personality cult."

If a faction can maintain complete control after their leader's death **and** choose to crown the same player's new character, that demonstrates:


- The faction is real, not a personality cult
- Institutional structures survived the death
- Loyalty transfers to the organization, not just the individual
- The player earned that loyalty through past actions

**This is dynastic/factional control** â€” exactly the kind of power consolidation that should be possible (and challengeable).


### The Critical Restriction

The philosophy establishes one hard rule: **"No alts holding power simultaneously."**

This means:


- âœ… **Sequential** â€” Player 1 dies, then Player 1's new character is crowned later = ALLOWED
- âŒ **Simultaneous** â€” Player 1 is King while also controlling Marshal with an alt = FORBIDDEN

As long as there's true death and succession between characters, the system is working as intended.


### Why This Is Politically Vulnerable

While mechanically allowed, this scenario creates **massive political exposure**:


> **Note**
> **Vulnerabilities of "Resurrection Rulership"**
>
> - **Delegitimization:** "He's just a puppet! The faction controls everything, not the King!"
> - **Target Identification:** Everyone knows WHO to assassinate to break faction control
> - **Corruption Narrative:** This becomes perfect ammunition for reformers and rival factions
> - **Weak Legitimacy:** "He died once and they crowned him again? He'll die again soon."
> - **Chronicle Tracking:** Public records show "Third coronation of [Player Name]" â€” pattern becomes obvious
> - **Internal Fracture Risk:** Faction members may question why they're serving the same person again


### Player Responses to Resurrection Politics

This scenario is **designed to provoke conflict**. Here's how players can respond:


| Response Type | Method | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Public Challenge | Declare the King a faction puppet, demand reform | Damages legitimacy, rallies opposition |
| Targeted Assassination | Kill the "resurrected" King again to prove pattern | If successful, undermines faction's ability to protect their chosen leader |
| Counter-Faction | Form rival faction with different values ("Merit over dynasties") | Creates institutional competition |
| Internal Subversion | Recruit disgruntled faction members tired of serving same person | Fractures the power base from within |
| Chronicle Weaponization | Point to public death records showing repeated resurrections | Makes pattern undeniable, fuels reform movements |
| Reformer Campaign | Run for King as a claimant promising "real change, not puppet kings" | Offers alternative legitimacy narrative |


### Historical Parallels: Dynastic Resilience

This scenario mirrors real historical patterns:


> **Note**
> **The Medici Returns (15th-16th Century)**
>
> The Medici family was expelled from Florence **twice** (1494 and 1527), yet returned to power both times because their institutional network (banking, church connections, noble alliances) remained intact. Individual Medicis died or were exiled, but the **family/faction** reconsolidated power.
> Opponents called them puppet masters and tyrants â€” yet they kept winning because their organizational control was real.


> **Note**
> **The Restoration Monarchies**
>
> English monarchy was abolished in 1649 (Charles I beheaded). In 1660, **his son** Charles II was restored to the throne. Same dynasty, different person. Why? The institutional support (nobility, church, military) never fully shifted. The **system** wanted monarchy back.
> This wasn't "resurrection" â€” it was institutional resilience.


> **Note**
> **Soviet Nomenklatura**
>
> Individual Soviet leaders died (Stalin 1953, Khrushchev ousted 1964, Brezhnev died 1982), but the **same institutional network** (party members, KGB, military officers) controlled succession. Different faces, same power structure. The **organization** outlived individuals.

In all cases: **Individuals died. Institutions survived. Power persisted.**


### When Does This Become a Problem?

This scenario only becomes problematic if **players stop responding to it**. If:


- No one assassinates the "resurrected" King
- No one forms rival factions
- No one challenges faction dominance
- No one weaponizes the Chronicle against them

Then the faction **earned** that dominance through player inaction. The system didn't fail â€” the opposition did.

## Building Power Outside Britain

Claimants usually (though not exclusively) build their base of power **outside of Britain city proper**. This is exactly what makes them so dangerous â€” they're able to organize in secret, right under the King's nose, and eventually make an attempt on the throne.

This is why claimant strategy is deeply relevant to the [[Guild System|Guilds]] and [[Faction System|Emergent-Factions]]. Claimants build their power base through guilds (the sovereign organizational structure), and their guild may also align with a faction (a voluntary identity layer on top of guilds). Eventually they attempt to take (or fail to take) the throne.

**But here's the twist:** They don't always stay outside. A successful Claimant might **first secure a court position** (Marshal, Magistrate, Regent) while **simultaneously leading a guild that is aligned with a faction**. This gives them both internal legitimacy and external power â€” the perfect combination for a successful coup.


### Why Outside Bases Were Common


#### 1. Exile = Safety + Freedom to Organize

If you stayed inside the contested territory, you risked arrest, assassination, or being politically boxed in. Outside Britain's walls, you could:


- Raise troops
- Gather money
- Build alliances
- Plan without constant surveillance

**Exile wasn't weakness â€” it was often a strategic reset.**


#### 2. Foreign Legitimacy and Resources

External powers (factions, guilds, outlying settlements) could provide:


- Armies
- Ships
- Funding
- Diplomatic recognition

That recognition alone could flip internal loyalties once the claimant returned.


#### 3. Distance Creates Mystique

Being absent sometimes helped. Claimants could:


- Avoid blame for current suffering
- Be idealized as a "rightful ruler"
- Let dissatisfaction inside the territory ripen on its own

## How Claimants Challenge Succession

Claimants have several strategies to claim the Crown:


### 1. Race to Coronation

The simplest path: **get to the coronation site first** and crown yourself before the Heir or other rivals.


- **Risk:** The Regent or Heir may block access to the coronation
- **Risk:** Being crowned without legitimacy may trigger civil war
- **Reward:** If successful, you become King â€” even if contested


> **â— Note**


### 2. Assassinate the Heir

If the official Heir is killed, their claim is **voided**. This clears the path for Claimants to:


- Step up as the next strongest claim
- Race to coronation without opposition
- Negotiate with the Regent for support


### 3. Build Alliances

Claimants can gain support by:


- **Negotiating with the Regent:** "Support my claim, and I'll keep you in power"
- **Recruiting noble houses:** Gain vassal loyalty through promises of land, titles, or wealth
- **Public declarations:** Announce their claim in Discord or in-game, rallying supporters


### 4. Military Uprising

If diplomatic paths fail, Claimants may:


- Raise armies to challenge the Heir or Regent
- Siege the coronation site to claim the Crown by force
- Start a civil war to contest succession


> Historical Parallel:This mirrors real-world succession conflicts like the Wars of the Roses, where rival claimants (Lancaster vs. York) fought for decades over who had the rightful claim to the English throne.


### 5. Delegitimize the Heir

Claimants may use **political warfare** to undermine the Heir's legitimacy:


- "The previous King was a tyrant â€” his appointed Heir has no right to rule"
- "The Heir is not of true blood â€” I am the rightful descendant"
- "The Heir is weak and unfit â€” I am the stronger candidate"

## Defending Against Claimants

If you are the **Heir** or **Regent**, you must deal with Claimants:


### As the Heir:


- **Crown yourself quickly:** The faster you complete coronation, the less time Claimants have to act
- **Secure alliances:** Rally noble houses and the Regent (if possible) to your side
- **Bodyguards & protection:** Claimants may assassinate you â€” stay vigilant
- **Delegitimize Claimants:** Publicly denounce them as traitors or pretenders


### As the Regent:


- **Support the Heir:** Crush Claimants militarily or politically
- **Negotiate with Claimants:** Offer them lesser titles to stand down
- **Self-crown:** If Claimants threaten stability, the Regent may seize the throne themselves
- **Execute Claimants:** Use Regent authority to imprison or eliminate rivals


## Types of Claims

Not all claims are equal. The **strength of a claim** affects how much political support a Claimant receives:


### 1. Strong Claims


- **Direct lineage:** Child or sibling of the previous King
- **Historical dynasty:** Descendant of a past ruling family
- **Popular support:** Widely loved or respected by the realm

*Strong claims gain more allies and legitimacy faster.*


### 2. Weak Claims


- **Distant relatives:** Cousins, distant lineage, or vague connections
- **Manufactured claims:** "I bought this title" or "I won a contest"
- **No lineage:** Pure merit or conquest-based claims

*Weak claims require more force, politics, or cunning to succeed.*


### 3. Fabricated Claims


- **False lineage:** Claiming heritage that doesn't exist
- **Forged documents:** Creating fake evidence of royal descent
- **Opportunistic claims:** "I found a crown, so I'm King now"

*Fabricated claims are the weakest and most likely to be rejected or crushed.*
