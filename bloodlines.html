Competing Theories of Legitimacy

What Are Bloodlines?

Bloodlines are not roles ‚Äî they are competing answers to why anyone should rule at all.

Each bloodline embodies a different theory of legitimacy. They all want the same thing ‚Äî influence over the crown, succession, law, violence, and the future of Britain. They just believe they deserve it for different reasons.

This creates permanent rivalry. Bloodlines don't operate in parallel lanes with different jobs. They collide constantly because they're fighting for the same prize.

Bloodlines exist to embody rival answers to why anyone should rule at all.

A Theory of Legitimacy Answers One Question:

"Why should anyone accept your rule even when they don't like you?"

Power explains who can rule today.
Legitimacy explains why rule survives tomorrow.

Bloodlines don't give you power. They let you justify having it. And in a permadeath world where every death asks "who's next, and why them?" ‚Äî legitimacy is the only thing that prevents politics from collapsing into pure violence.

The Core Purpose

Bloodlines solve three hard problems simultaneously:

  1. Who is allowed to claim ultimate authority?
  2. Why does killing this person matter more than killing anyone else?
  3. How does the world remember history after characters die?

No other system answers all three cleanly.

Why Bloodlines Exist in a Permadeath World

Permadeath does something brutal to politics: key players die, alliances vanish overnight, conflicts resolve too fast, history resets, and new characters have no context. Without a stabilizing structure, you get short spikes of drama followed by total reset on repeat.

Bloodlines are the glue that keeps political intrigue from collapsing every time someone dies.

What Bloodlines Actually Glue Together

Bloodlines provide continuity without immortality. They connect:

  • Past decisions ‚Üí future consequences: A bloodline's choices create enemies and allies that outlast individual characters
  • Dead characters ‚Üí living claims: When someone dies, their goals don't vanish ‚Äî the next seat inherits suspicion, allies, enemies, and expectations
  • Old grudges ‚Üí new players: "The Hawkwrights always betray allies" becomes institutional memory
  • Unresolved conflicts ‚Üí new flashpoints: Disputes don't disappear with death; they transform and escalate

Bloodlines ensure that death ends characters, not stories. They turn permadeath from a reset button into a story accelerant.

How Bloodlines Prevent Power From Becoming Trivial

Without bloodlines in a permadeath world:

  • The strongest group always wins
  • Politics becomes just PvP
  • Intrigue gives way to dominance
  • Players stop investing emotionally because nothing lasts

With bloodlines:

  • Victory must be justified, not just achieved
  • Coups remain unstable even after winning
  • Authority is always contested
  • Political tension outlives the people who created it

Bloodlines slow the system down just enough to let intrigue breathe, while still preserving the weight of permadeath.

The Five Theories of Legitimacy

All bloodlines want the same thing: influence over Britain. They differ only in how they justify deserving it.

Every major event becomes a theory conflict, not a mechanics check. Same event ‚Äî five incompatible answers. That's endless intrigue.

‚öñÔ∏è Legitimacy by Law (Hawkwright)

Core belief: Rule must be documented. Succession must be continuous. Ceremony matters. Precedent binds the present.

What it fears: Chaos, exceptionalism, "just this once" decisions

During crisis: "We don't need a strong ruler. We need a recognized one."

Why it survives deaths: Because law outlives people.

Weakness: Slow, vulnerable to force, can be bypassed in emergencies.

Rivals: Rowan (force vs process), Blackthorn (documents vs blood)

Alternative Surname Ideas: Hawkwright, Sealby, Writford, Charteris, Ledgerham, Markham, Bellcourt, Verwick, Notley, Cleremont

Fictional Parallels:

  • Game of Thrones: House Arryn (obsessed with oaths and legal succession), The Citadel Maesters (continuity through knowledge), The Small Council at its best (procedure over personality)
  • Tolkien: The Stewards of Gondor pre-Denethor (authority via stewardship and record), The White Tree as symbol (legitimacy acknowledged through institution)
  • Other Fantasy: Imperial bureaucratic families (Elder Scrolls), The Chantry's clerical dynasties (Dragon Age), Noble jurist families (Discworld)

Instinctive player read: "If the records don't recognize you, you don't exist."

üõ°Ô∏è Legitimacy by Force (Rowan)

What it fears: Hesitation, fragmentation, mob rule

During crisis: "Talk later. Secure the streets now."

Why it survives deaths: Because force reorganizes quickly.

Weakness: Breeds resentment, needs constant enforcement, creates martyrs.

  • Tolkien: Saruman's captains (stability through domination), The Mouth of Sauron (authority backed by terror)
  • Other Fantasy: The Empire (Star Wars - peace through overwhelming force), Templar dynasties (Dragon Age), Redguard warlords (Elder Scrolls)

Instinctive player read: "When the city burns, people stop asking who should rule and start asking who can stop this."

What it fears: Elitism, closed courts, secret deals

During crisis: "If the city won't accept them, they cannot rule."

Why it survives deaths: Because people keep existing.

Weakness: Unstable, easily manipulated, turns volatile fast.

Fictional Parallels:

  • Game of Thrones: The Faith Militant / Sparrows (popular outrage ‚Üí real power, legitimacy through mass belief), King's Landing mob leaders
  • Tolkien: Men rallying to Aragorn (destiny requires acceptance), The Scouring of the Shire (popular resistance reshapes power)
  • Other Fantasy: Free City demagogues, Guild revolutions (Discworld), Commons-driven councils (Witcher lore)

Instinctive player read: "If the streets turn against you, nothing else matters."

What it fears: Usurpers, "new men," precedent-breaking

Fictional Parallels:

  • Game of Thrones: House Targaryen (blood = destiny), House Stark early (ancient right to rule the North), Baratheon claimants (even usurpers rely on blood arguments)
  • Tolkien: Aragorn / Isildur's line (kingship as fate), The N√∫men√≥rean bloodline (diminished but still special)
  • Other Fantasy: Dragonlord families (various settings), Chosen bloodlines (Wheel of Time), Elder bloodlines (The Witcher)

Instinctive player read: "You can take the throne ‚Äî but it will never be yours."

Core belief: Hypocrisy destroys power. Secrets invalidate rule. No one deserves authority unexamined.

What it fears: Silence, forgetting, narrative control

During crisis: "Before you choose a ruler ‚Äî know what they've done."

Why it survives deaths: Because truth persists once revealed.

Weakness: Paralysis, cynicism, endless scandal.

Rivals: Everyone ‚Äî they don't rule, they decide who can't.

Alternative Surname Ideas: Greymark, Halloway, Ashwell, Candlewick, Whitlock, Graves, Morland, Pryce, Vale, Crowley

Fictional Parallels:

  • Game of Thrones: Varys (power through secrets), Littlefinger partially (exposure as leverage), Samwell Tarly late (truth destabilizes rule)

Critical Design Rule: No Theory Can Permanently Eliminate the Others

If Force wipes out Law forever ‚Üí tyranny
If Law freezes Force ‚Üí paralysis
If Popular Will dominates ‚Üí chaos
If Blood dominates ‚Üí stagnation
If Truth dominates ‚Üí nihilism

Balance comes from coexistence through conflict, not equilibrium.

How Theories Clash: Example Crisis

Event: King Assassinated
  • Hawkwright: "Who is the lawful successor?"
  • Rowan: "Who can secure Britain tonight?"
  • Ashcroft: "Who will the city accept?"
  • Blackthorn: "Who has the blood claim?"
  • Greymark: "Who ordered the murder?"

Same event. Five incompatible answers. That's the game.

Bloodline Seats (Not Family Trees)

Bloodlines are not modeled as family trees. They are a finite set of playable seats that represent generations.

Each bloodline has:

  • A surname and reputation
  • A lore blurb
  • A set of character seats (playable identities)

Players "jump into" a seat, not a tree. At character selection, players see available vacant seats and claim one.

What Happens When a Seat Dies?

When a bloodline character dies:

  1. The seat becomes vacant
  2. A mourning period occurs (e.g., 48 hours)
  3. The seat may reopen as a successor: "Alden Hawkwright, Heir of Cedric"
Important: The person does not return. The seat transforms. Same bloodline, same seat index, but a new identity with different hooks.

Bloodline Growth

Bloodlines start small (1-2 seats) and only grow when the world justifies it:

  • Royal Recognition: Crown legitimizes an heir or cadet branch
  • Legal Recognition: Court establishes lineage or adoption
  • Land Grant: Bloodline gains permanent land
  • Survival Under Fire: Bloodline survives a major purge

Bloodline Extinction & Replacement

Bloodlines can die out. When a bloodline goes extinct:

  • The name disappears permanently
  • Politics shift tone
  • The city feels the loss

Important: Theories Don't Die

The theory of legitimacy does not disappear when the bloodline does.

If House Hawkwright (Legitimacy by Law) goes extinct, the need for legal legitimacy doesn't vanish. Eventually, a new bloodline embodying that theory will emerge ‚Äî under a different name, perhaps through different origins, but representing the same fundamental answer to "who deserves to rule."

How replacement occurs: Through world-shaping events. A new bloodline might be recognized after:

  • A commoner rises to king and is ennobled, founding a new legitimist house
  • A foreign power backs a claimant who embodies law-based rule
  • A reformation movement creates a "Second Hawkwright" lineage
  • The Crown deliberately recreates the vacuum by elevating someone

The exact mechanism is emergent and story-driven, not mechanical. Staff recognizes when the world has produced a natural successor to an extinct theory.

How Players Recognize Bloodlines In-Game

Bloodline importance must be visible, procedural, and risky to ignore. A guard shouldn't need to "know" lore ‚Äî they should see it immediately.

Visual Signaling (Instant Recognition)

Overhead Title

Example display:

Alric Rowan
[Rowan Bloodline]
(Veteran Commander)

This is not cosmetic ‚Äî it's informational. Players see it and immediately know "this person carries recognized legitimacy."

Recognizable Insignia (Item-Based)

Each bloodline has one unmistakable visual token:

  • Rowan War Medallion
  • Hawkwright Signet Ring
  • Ashcroft Merchant Seal

Guards are trained (IC) to recognize these. If a player sees the item: "This person carries recognized legitimacy."

Speech/Name Hue (Subtle)

Not bright neon ‚Äî restrained, official:

  • Rowan: iron/steel gray
  • Hawkwright: parchment gold

This trains pattern recognition subconsciously.

Procedural Authority (Why Guards Obey)

Bloodline characters do NOT issue direct commands like "Arrest him." They invoke process triggers.

Example: Rowan Invoking Authority

Alric Rowan says: "By my authority as Rowan, this situation is unstable. Guards ‚Äî contain and hold."

That's not a command. That's a procedural escalation.

What the guard player understands instantly:

  • This is someone whose judgment will be reviewed
  • Ignoring them could get me blamed later, charged with dereliction, or on the wrong side of future power
  • So the guard thinks: "I can disagree later. But ignoring this now is dangerous."

That's realism. Guards obey because it's safer, not because it's forced.

Social Consequence (The Real Enforcement)

If a guard ignores a bloodline member, nothing explodes immediately. Instead:

  • Chroniclers write it down
  • Commanders ask questions
  • The guard gets labeled "unreliable"
  • Later: promotions stall, excluded from sensitive operations, quietly blamed when things go wrong

No mechanics needed. Just memory and reputation.

When a bloodline speaks, you listen ‚Äî not because they command you,
but because history will ask why you didn't.

What Bloodlines Are NOT

  • ‚ùå Factions or guilds
  • ‚ùå Skill trees or progression paths
  • ‚ùå Sources of combat bonuses
  • ‚ùå Exclusive clubs requiring invitations

Bloodlines don't give you things. They give you problems.

A bloodline character is not more powerful ‚Äî they are more consequential. When they act or die, the world is forced to react.

üîß Hard Mechanics

Bloodline Seat Registry

  • Data structure: Each bloodline stored with BloodlineId, Name, TheoryType enum, ActiveSeats list
  • Seat limits: Soft cap 3 seats, hard cap 5 seats per bloodline
  • Seat states: VACANT | OCCUPIED | MOURNING | EXTINCT
  • Visual markers: Bloodline members have overhead title "[Bloodline Name]" with unique hue

Character Selection Integration

  • Role selection gump: Shows bloodline seats with status (VACANT/TAKEN)
  • Claim validation: Check account doesn't already hold another bloodline seat
  • Template application: On claim, apply fixed name, body hue, starting items, spawn location
  • Bloodline tag: Mobile.BloodlineId set, tracked server-wide

Death & Succession

  • On permadeath: Seat enters MOURNING state for 48 hours (configurable)
  • During mourning: Seat is locked, cannot be claimed
  • After mourning: Seat reopens with modified template (new name, "Heir of X")
  • Renewal limits: Each seat can renew 2-3 times before extinction risk

Visibility & Recognition

  • Name display: "[Bloodline] Name" format with colored bloodline tag
  • Insignia items: Each bloodline has unique wearable signet/medallion (blessed, non-transferable)
  • Chronicle integration: Bloodline deaths auto-logged with special formatting
  • Guard behavior: Guards recognize bloodline members via tag check, adjust responses

üíª Pseudocode & Implementation

Bloodline Data Structure

public class Bloodline
{
    public string BloodlineId { get; set; }  // "Hawkwright"
    public string DisplayName { get; set; }  // "House Hawkwright"
    public TheoryType Theory { get; set; }   // Law, Force, PopularWill, etc.
    public int SoftCap { get; set; } = 3;
    public int HardCap { get; set; } = 5;
    
    public List<BloodlineSeat> Seats { get; set; }
    
    public BloodlineSeat GetVacantSeat()
    {
        return Seats.FirstOrDefault(s => s.State == SeatState.VACANT);
    }
    
    public bool IsExtinct()
    {
        return Seats.All(s => s.State == SeatState.EXTINCT);
    }
}

public class BloodlineSeat
{
    public string SeatId { get; set; }         // "Hawkwright_Patriarch"
    public string TemplateName { get; set; }   // "Cedric Hawkwright"
    public SeatState State { get; set; }
    public Mobile CurrentHolder { get; set; }
    public DateTime MourningUntil { get; set; }
    public int RenewalCount { get; set; }
    public int MaxRenewals { get; set; } = 2;
    
    public void OnDeath()
    {
        State = SeatState.MOURNING;
        MourningUntil = DateTime.UtcNow.AddHours(48);
        CurrentHolder = null;
        RenewalCount++;
    }
    
    public bool CanRenew()
    {
        return RenewalCount < MaxRenewals;
    }
}

Character Selection Integration

public class BloodlineSelectionGump : Gump
{
    public override void OnResponse(Mobile from, GumpInfo info)
    {
        int buttonId = info.ButtonID;
        
        // Map button to bloodline seat
        var seat = BloodlineRegistry.GetSeatByButtonId(buttonId);
        
        if (seat == null || seat.State != SeatState.VACANT)
        {
            from.SendMessage("That seat is not available.");
            return;
        }
        
        // Validate: one bloodline per account
        if (BloodlineRegistry.AccountHasBloodline(from.Account))
        {
            from.SendMessage("Your account already holds a bloodline seat.");
            return;
        }
        
        // Apply template
        BloodlineTemplate template = seat.GetTemplate();
        template.ApplyTo(from); // Sets name, body, hue, skills, items
        
        // Assign seat
        seat.CurrentHolder = from;
        seat.State = SeatState.OCCUPIED;
        from.BloodlineId = seat.Bloodline.BloodlineId;
        
        // Visual marker
        from.AddItem(new BloodlineInsignia(seat.Bloodline));
        
        // Log to Chronicle
        Chronicle.RecordBloodlineClaim(from, seat);
    }
}

Death & Mourning System

public static void OnBloodlineDeath(Mobile victim)
{
    var seat = BloodlineRegistry.GetSeatByMobile(victim);
    if (seat == null) return;
    
    // Record to Chronicle with special formatting
    Chronicle.RecordBloodlineDeath(victim, seat.Bloodline);
    
    // Enter mourning
    seat.OnDeath();
    
    // Check for extinction
    if (!seat.CanRenew())
    {
        seat.State = SeatState.EXTINCT;
        
        if (seat.Bloodline.IsExtinct())
        {
            // Bloodline-wide extinction event
            OnBloodlineExtinction(seat.Bloodline);
        }
    }
    
    // Schedule mourning end
    Timer.DelayCall(TimeSpan.FromHours(48), () => 
    {
        if (seat.State == SeatState.MOURNING && seat.CanRenew())
        {
            seat.State = SeatState.VACANT;
            // Modify template for successor
            seat.TemplateName = $"Heir of {victim.Name}";
            Chronicle.AddEntry($"{seat.Bloodline.DisplayName} mourning ends");
        }
    });
}

‚ö†Ô∏è Possible Problems & Conflicts

Problem: Bloodline Hoarding

Symptom: One group controls all seats in a bloodline via coordination

Solution: Hard limit: one bloodline seat per account. No exceptions. Use IP checks for multi-account abuse detection. Make bloodline members natural rivals through competing interests even within same house.

Problem: Meaningless Bloodlines

Symptom: Players claim bloodlines but don't embody the theory; bloodlines become cosmetic

Solution: Staff quietly note inactive/off-theory bloodline play. Chronicler can publicly question legitimacy. Other bloodlines can challenge "are they really upholding Law/Force/etc?" Make consequences social, not mechanical punishments.

Problem: Bloodline Extinction Too Easy/Hard

Symptom: Either bloodlines die out week 1, or they're immortal despite bad play

Solution: Tunable renewal limits (start at 2-3 renewals per seat). Monitor extinction rates. If too fast, increase limits slightly. If never happens, decrease. Goldilocks zone: 1 extinction every 2-3 months feels momentous.

Problem: New Players Can't Get Bloodline Seats

Symptom: All seats perpetually occupied by veterans

Solution: Permadeath naturally creates openings. If this still becomes an issue, add "dormancy" rule: if a bloodline seat holder doesn't log in for 5+ days, seat can be challenged/vacated by staff. Prevents seat-parking.

‚ùì Questions & Answers

Q: Can I switch bloodlines?

A: No. Bloodline is identity, not a guild. If you claim Hawkwright, you are Hawkwright until death. Want a different bloodline? Your character must die.

Q: What if I'm the last member of my bloodline and I die?

A: If your seat was the last active seat and can't renew, the bloodline goes extinct. This is permanent and shifts the political landscape. Your death becomes legendary ‚Äî "the one who ended House Hawkwright."

When a bloodline goes extinct:

  • The name disappears permanently from Britain
  • The theory that bloodline represented (Law, Force, Popular Will, Blood, or Truth) does not disappear
  • Politics shift tone ‚Äî guards may fracture, legal continuity breaks, or mob rule emerges depending on which bloodline fell
  • Eventually, a new bloodline embodying that theory may be recognized through world-shaping events

How replacement occurs: A new bloodline might emerge after a commoner rises to king and is ennobled, a foreign power backs a new claimant, or a reformation movement creates a successor lineage. This is emergent and story-driven ‚Äî staff recognizes when the world has produced a natural successor to an extinct theory. See Bloodline Extinction & Replacement section above for details.

Q: Can bloodlines be created by players?

A: Not initially. Season 1 starts with 3-5 fixed bloodlines (Hawkwright, Rowan, Ashcroft recommended). New bloodlines can emerge later through major events: a king ennobling a hero, a rebellion creating a new house, etc. This is staff-approved, not player-initiated.

Q: Do bloodline members get stat bonuses or special abilities?

A: Absolutely not. Bloodlines grant zero mechanical advantages. No stat boosts, no special skills, no combat buffs. You get legitimacy and visibility. That's it. If you want power, earn it through play.

Q: What happens if two bloodline members fight each other?

A: That's internal bloodline conflict ‚Äî excellent drama. The bloodline doesn't protect you from your own. In fact, succession disputes within bloodlines are some of the best stories. Guards treat it as they would any violence (context-dependent).

Q: Why are bloodlines limited to 3-5 seats?

A: Scarcity creates value. If bloodlines have unlimited seats, death becomes meaningless and legitimacy dilutes. Small numbers keep each seat precious, each death impactful, and politics concentrated.

Q: Can I be a commoner who later becomes bloodline?

A: Not by "becoming" bloodline mid-character. However, if you distinguish yourself enough, you can have a bloodline created through RP processes (as long as there is an open spot for a new bloodline). This typically requires world-shaping events like being ennobled by the Crown, winning a civil war, or embodying a needed theory of legitimacy. See Bloodline Extinction & Replacement for how new bloodlines emerge.
