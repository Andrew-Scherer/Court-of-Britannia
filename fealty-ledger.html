Core Principle

The Fealty Ledger (also called the Treaty Ledger or Recognition Ledger) is the public, permanent record of all political relationships between cities.

It exists to create accountability through visibility.

The Ledger records:

  • ‚úÖ Fealty relationships (liege/vassal bonds)
  • ‚úÖ Treaties (alliances, neutrality pacts, trade agreements)
  • ‚úÖ Recognition statuses (which cities recognize which capitals)
  • ‚úÖ Tribute payments (amount and frequency)
  • ‚úÖ Treaty violations and breaches
  • ‚úÖ Treaty terminations and expirations

The Ledger does NOT:

  • ‚ùå Enforce treaties mechanically
  • ‚ùå Judge which side is "right" in disputes
  • ‚ùå Prevent betrayal or treaty violations
  • ‚ùå Hide information from players
Design Victory: Transparency creates political consequences without needing enforcement scripts.

What Gets Logged

1. Fealty Relationships

Example Ledger Entry

FEALTY SWORN
Date: January 15, 1485
Liege: Britain
Vassal: Yew
Tribute Rate: 15% of tax revenue
Terms: Protection, warrant recognition, trade privilege
Duration: Indefinite
Signed By: King Edmund of Britain, Lord Garrett of Yew

Logged Information:

  • Date and time of signing
  • Parties involved (liege and vassal)
  • Tribute percentage
  • Promises and obligations
  • Duration (indefinite, fixed term, conditional)
  • Signatories (who agreed)

2. General Treaties

Example Ledger Entry

TREATY SIGNED: MUTUAL DEFENSE PACT
Date: February 3, 1485
Parties: Vesper, Minoc
Terms: Mutual military support if attacked by third party
Exclusions: Does not apply if either attacks first
Duration: 2 years (expires February 3, 1487)
Signed By: Mayor Elara of Vesper, Guildmaster Thrain of Minoc

Treaty types logged include:

  • Alliances (mutual defense)
  • Neutrality pacts (non-aggression)
  • Trade agreements
  • Recognition treaties
  • Extradition agreements
  • Tribute arrangements (without fealty)

See: Inter-City Treaties for full treaty types

3. Capital Recognition

Example Ledger Entry

CAPITAL RECOGNITION DECLARED
Date: March 8, 1485
City: Yew
Recognizes: Britain as Capital
Legitimacy Theory: Continuity (Britain is always the rightful seat)
Declared By: Lord Garrett of Yew

Tracks ideological alignment:

  • Which cities recognize which capital
  • Legitimacy theory invoked (Continuity, Strength, Mandate, Autonomy)
  • Date of recognition or revocation

See: Capital Recognition for ideology mechanics

4. Tribute Payments

Example Ledger Entry

TRIBUTE PAID
Date: March 15, 1485
From: Yew
To: Britain
Amount: 1,247 gold (15% of 8,313 gold weekly tax revenue)
Status: On time

Tracks financial flows:

  • Amount transferred
  • Percentage of vassal's tax revenue
  • Payment frequency (weekly, monthly)
  • On-time or late status

See: Inter-City Tithes for tribute mechanics

5. Treaty Violations

Example Ledger Entry

TREATY BREACH DECLARED
Date: April 22, 1485
Accuser: Britain
Accused: Yew
Treaty: Fealty Agreement (signed January 15, 1485)
Alleged Violation: Harbored fugitive (Sir Duncan, wanted for treason)
Evidence: Witness testimony, guard logs
Response: Yew denies breach, claims Duncan is political refugee

Creates permanent dispute record:

  • Who accused whom
  • What treaty was allegedly violated
  • Specific act claimed as breach
  • Evidence presented
  • Accused's response (if any)
Critical: Breach declarations are logged even if disputed.
The system does not judge who is right. It only records the accusation.

6. Treaty Terminations

Example Ledger Entry

FEALTY BROKEN
Date: May 1, 1485
Method: Formal Withdrawal
Vassal: Yew
Liege: Britain
Reason Stated: "Excessive tribute demands unsustainable"
Response: Britain declares Yew in rebellion, withdraws protection

End-of-relationship records:

  • How the treaty ended (formal, hostile, expiration)
  • Who initiated termination
  • Reason given (if any)
  • Other party's response

See: Secession, Neutrality, and Rebellion

Who Can Access the Ledger

The Fealty Ledger is completely public and accessible to all players.

In-Game Access Points

Players can view the ledger through multiple interfaces:

  • City Hall Book: Physical ledger book in each city's hall
  • Herald Announcements: Major events broadcast realm-wide
  • Command Interface: [viewledger command for instant access
  • Website Dashboard: Out-of-game web view (optional)

Filter & Search Options

Players can filter ledger entries by:

  • City: Show all relationships involving specific city
  • Treaty Type: Fealty, alliance, trade, etc.
  • Status: Active, expired, broken
  • Date Range: View historical entries
  • Player/Ruler: Show treaties signed by specific person

Example Query

Command: [viewledger britain fealty active

Result: Shows all current fealty relationships where Britain is liege or vassal

Why Complete Visibility Matters

1. Accountability

Public logging creates social pressure:

  • Breach accusations become permanent record
  • Players can see who violated what
  • Reputation accumulates over time
  • Future treaty partners can research history
If you betray one ally, all future allies will know.

2. No Hidden Deals

Transparency prevents admin favoritism and closed-door corruption:

  • No secret treaties between staff and specific players
  • All agreements equally visible
  • Community can audit any relationship

3. Emergent Narrative

The ledger becomes the historical record of the realm:

  • Players can look back at old alliances
  • See when Britain dominated vs. when it was weak
  • Trace rise and fall of power blocs
  • Document famous betrayals and heroic loyalties

4. Intelligence Gathering

Smart players use the ledger strategically:

  • Identify potential allies: Who is isolated?
  • Detect vulnerability: Which cities recently lost protection?
  • Spot instability: Which treaties are near expiration?
  • Research reliability: Does this city honor agreements?

Privacy vs. Visibility Trade-offs

What Is NOT Logged

Some information remains private to maintain negotiation space:

  • Negotiation content: Only final signed terms are public
  • Rejected proposals: If a treaty offer is refused, it's not logged
  • Private conversations: In-person discussions stay private
  • Internal city politics: Appointments, salaries, local laws

Design Balance

Public: Final agreements, tribute flows, breaches, terminations

Private: Negotiation process, internal debate, rejected offers

Why: Players need room to negotiate without every proposal becoming ammunition

Historical Record

The Fealty Ledger never deletes entries. All records are permanent.

Why Permanence Matters

  • Reputation is earned over time: Cannot erase past betrayals
  • Political cycles visible: See when cities were weak, strong, allied, hostile
  • Succession disputes: "When King Edmund ruled, we had peace..."
  • Myth-building: "The Great Betrayal of 1485" becomes server legend

Admin Tools (Read-Only)

Server administrators have no special powers over the ledger except:

  • Manual corrections: Fix typos or technical errors (logged publicly)
  • Annotations: Add context notes (marked as admin commentary)
  • Archive view: Access very old entries more easily
Critical: Admins cannot delete, hide, or retroactively alter ledger entries.
If a correction is needed, it is logged as a new entry referencing the original.

Common Questions

Q: Can I hide my treaties from enemies?

A: No. All signed treaties are public. You can negotiate privately, but once signed, it's visible to everyone.

Q: What if someone falsely accuses me of breach?

A: The accusation gets logged. You can respond with your own ledger entry disputing it. The community sees both sides and judges for themselves.

Q: Can I delete old embarrassing treaties?

A: No. All ledger entries are permanent. Your political history follows you.

Q: How far back does the ledger go?

A: Indefinite. The ledger has existed since the city-state system launched and will never reset.

Q: Can I propose a treaty without it being logged?

A: Yes. Proposals are private. Only finalized, signed treaties appear in the ledger.

Q: What if my city deactivates? Do treaties disappear?

A: Treaties are marked as "Suspended (City Inactive)" but remain in the ledger. If your city reactivates, treaties can resume or be renegotiated.
