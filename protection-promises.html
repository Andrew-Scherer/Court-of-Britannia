When Oaths Break Under Pressure

Protection is not a button. It is a promise.
And every promise can be broken.

Core Principle

When a vassal swears fealty, the liege promises protection. This is the core bargain: tribute for safety.

But protection is NOT:

  • ‚ùå Automatically triggered by system
  • ‚ùå Guaranteed to arrive in time
  • ‚ùå Mechanically enforced
  • ‚ùå Immune to political calculation

Protection IS:

  • ‚úÖ A political commitment that must be acted upon
  • ‚úÖ Subject to liege's discretion and capability
  • ‚úÖ Publicly visible (or publicly absent)
  • ‚úÖ The primary justification for tribute
  • ‚úÖ The most explosive point of failure in feudal contracts
Design Victory: Protection failure creates justifiable rebellion without scripted war mechanics.

What Protection Means

The Promise

When a liege accepts a vassal's fealty, they implicitly or explicitly promise:

  • Military support when attacked by external forces
  • Guard assistance for law enforcement or defense
  • Diplomatic intervention on vassal's behalf
  • Economic aid during crises (optional, depends on treaty)
  • Recognition of vassal's sovereignty against rival claimants

What Protection Does NOT Include

Vassal attacked by external city ‚úÖ Yes ‚Äî Core protection obligation

How Protection Should Work (Ideal)

Vassal Under Attack

Scenario: Yew Calls for Help

  1. Yew is sieged by Vesper (external aggressor)
  2. Yew's ruler notifies Britain (their liege): "We need immediate aid"
  3. Britain mobilizes response:
    • Sends guards to Yew
    • Rallies other vassals to defend Yew
    • Threatens Vesper with retaliation
    • Provides emergency gold if needed
  4. Vesper backs down or faces Britain's wrath
  5. Yew's faith in fealty confirmed

Result: Protection promise fulfilled. Fealty strengthened.

This is how the system is supposed to work. But it requires:

  • Liege is online and responsive
  • Liege has military capacity
  • Liege considers vassal worth protecting
  • Liege is not already engaged elsewhere

When Protection Fails

Types of Failure

Failure Type Description Vassal Reaction
Ignores Request Liege receives plea for help, does nothing Outrage, justifiable secession
Too Slow Liege responds, but arrives after vassal is sacked Resentment, "where were you?"
Insufficient Force Liege sends token aid, vassal still falls Disappointment, questions commitment
Refuses to Help Liege explicitly says "We cannot help you" Breach of contract, secession trigger
Backs Attacker Liege sides with aggressor (ultimate betrayal) Immediate secession, rage

Why Lieges Fail to Protect

Common reasons a liege might not fulfill protection promises:

Legitimate Reasons

  • Overextended: Already fighting elsewhere, can't help both
  • Weak: Doesn't have military capacity to defend vassal
  • Offline: Key players unavailable when attack happens
  • Strategic priority: Protecting a different vassal deemed more important
  • Impossible timing: Attack happens too fast to respond

Cynical Reasons

  • Doesn't care: Vassal not valuable enough to risk resources
  • Wants vassal to fall: Secretly happy to see troublesome vassal weakened
  • Calculated abandonment: Lets vassal be destroyed to justify seizing territory
  • Distracted by internal politics: Too busy with court intrigue
  • Secretly allied with attacker: Coordinated betrayal
Critical: The system does not distinguish between legitimate and cynical reasons.
Both look the same to the abandoned vassal: protection failure.

Consequences of Protection Failure

Immediate Political Fallout

When a liege fails to protect a vassal:

  • Vassal publicly denounces failure (logged in Fealty Ledger)
  • Breach of contract declared by vassal
  • Justification for secession established
  • Other vassals take notice ("If Britain won't protect Yew, will they protect us?")
  • Liege's reputation damaged realm-wide

Vassal Response Options

Response Description When to Use
Formal Complaint Public declaration of breach, demand explanation Vassal wants answer but not ready to secede
Withhold Tribute Stop paying until protection guaranteed Economic pressure tactic
Demand Compensation Request gold to rebuild after attack Test liege's commitment
Immediate Secession Break fealty citing protection failure Bridge burned, justification clear
Seek New Liege Approach rival power with protection offer Betrayal move, maximum impact

Case Study: The Abandonment of Yew

Timeline of Failure

1486, June 1: Vesper raids Yew, kills guards, loots treasury

1486, June 1 (1 hour later): Yew's ruler sends urgent message to Britain: "We are under attack, send aid immediately"

1486, June 1 (3 hours later): No response from Britain

1486, June 2: Yew's ruler publishes open letter: "Where is Britain? We pay tribute for protection, yet stand alone."

1486, June 3: Britain responds: "We were engaged in our own matters. Yew should strengthen its own defenses."

1486, June 4: Yew declares breach of fealty contract

1486, June 5: Yew breaks fealty, publicly citing protection failure

1486, June 7: Yew approaches Vesper (the former attacker) and negotiates new fealty terms

Result:

  • Britain loses Yew as vassal (tribute stops)
  • Vesper gains Yew as vassal (tribute starts)
  • Britain's other vassals question reliability
  • Server views Britain as weak or untrustworthy
  • Realm power structure shifts
This scenario is politically explosive and entirely player-driven.

Multi-Vassal Protection Dilemmas

The Triage Problem

What happens when multiple vassals need help simultaneously?

Scenario: Britain's Impossible Choice

1487, March 15: Both Yew and Trinsic attacked on same day

Britain's Dilemma:

  • Can only send guards to one (insufficient forces)
  • Yew pays 15% tribute vs. Trinsic pays 20% tribute
  • Trinsic strategically more important (border defense)
  • Yew historically loyal vs. Trinsic recently problematic

Britain chooses Trinsic.

Consequences:

  • Yew feels abandoned despite years of loyalty
  • Yew breaks fealty citing protection failure
  • Trinsic feels prioritized, loyalty strengthens
  • Other vassals learn: "Be strategically important or be abandoned"

The Reputation Cascade

One protection failure can trigger mass defection:

  1. Britain fails to protect Yew
  2. Yew secedes publicly, citing breach
  3. Minoc watches and thinks: "If Britain couldn't protect Yew, they can't protect me"
  4. Minoc preemptively secedes before being attacked
  5. Trinsic demands lower tribute or threatens to leave
  6. Britain's realm collapses due to reputation damage

Justifying Breach vs. Fabricating Breach

Legitimate Breach Claims

Clear cases where vassal has justification:

  • Vassal called for help, liege did nothing
  • Liege explicitly refused to help
  • Liege sent insufficient aid after being warned
  • Liege backed the attacker
  • Liege took days to respond to urgent plea

Dubious Breach Claims

Cases where vassal is stretching justification:

  • Attack was over before liege could reasonably respond
  • Vassal started the fight, lost, then blamed liege
  • Liege sent aid but vassal refused to coordinate
  • Liege was offline during surprise attack
  • Vassal fabricates attack to justify leaving
The system does not judge validity.
Both sides tell their story publicly. The realm decides who to believe.

Liege Responses to Failure Accusations

Defensive Responses

Response Effect
"We were overextended" Admits failure but provides reason
"We sent aid but Yew refused to wait" Shifts blame to vassal
"Yew provoked the attack" Justifies non-intervention
"We cannot protect those who will not defend themselves" Criticizes vassal's weakness
"Yew is fabricating this to justify disloyalty" Attacks vassal's credibility

Offensive Responses

  • Preemptive termination: "If Yew doubts our protection, they are free to leave" (calls bluff)
  • Counter-accusation: "Yew has been withholding tribute" (shift focus)
  • Rally other vassals: "See how Yew abandons us in time of need" (isolation tactic)
  • Offer compensation: Gold or reduced tribute to retain vassal (damage control)

Treaties & Protection Clauses

Defining Protection in Advance

Smart lieges and vassals define protection explicitly in fealty treaties:

Example Protection Clause

Terms:

  • "Britain will respond to military threats within 24 hours"
  • "Response includes minimum 10 guards or equivalent force"
  • "Does not apply if Yew attacks first"
  • "Britain may request Yew coordinate defense timing"
  • "Failure to respond = breach of contract"

Benefit: Clear expectations reduce disputes.

See: Inter-City Treaties for treaty structure

When Protection Succeeds

Positive Reinforcement

When a liege successfully protects a vassal:

  • Vassal loyalty strengthens
  • Tribute seen as worthwhile
  • Other vassals feel safer
  • Liege's reputation improves
  • Secession threats decrease

Example: Britain Delivers

1488, January: Vesper attacks Yew

Within 2 hours: Britain sends 20 guards + King's personal guard

Result: Vesper retreats, Yew defended successfully

Impact:

  • Yew publicly thanks Britain
  • Yew's residents see value in tribute
  • Minoc and Trinsic feel secure
  • Vesper learns not to attack Britain's vassals
  • Britain consolidates power through demonstration

The Protection Paradox

Key Insight: The stronger a liege's reputation for protection, the less often they must actually provide it.

  • Strong reputation: Aggressors avoid attacking vassals ("Don't poke Britain's vassals ‚Äî they'll respond")
  • Weak reputation: Aggressors target vassals freely ("Britain won't help, free loot!")
Successful protection is deterrence. Failed protection is chaos.

Common Questions

Q: Can the liege auto-deploy guards to vassals under attack?

A: Not automatically. Protection requires manual player action ‚Äî sending guards, players, resources. This is intentional.

Q: What if I'm offline when my vassal is attacked?

A: This is a protection failure. Your vassal may view it as breach. Consider appointing responsive officials or allies.

Q: Can I terminate fealty if my liege fails to protect once?

A: Yes, mechanically you can terminate any time. Whether the realm views it as justified depends on circumstances.

Q: What if the attack was fake/staged by the vassal?

A: The liege can publicly accuse the vassal of fabrication. The realm decides who to believe based on evidence and reputation.

Q: Can I demand compensation for failed protection?

A: Yes. You can demand gold, reduced tribute, or other concessions. The liege can accept, negotiate, or refuse.

Q: If I break fealty due to protection failure, can the liege claim I'm in breach?

A: Yes. Both sides can claim breach. It becomes a public dispute where reputation and evidence matter.

Design Intent

Protection promise mechanics create:

  • Conditional loyalty ‚Äî Vassals stay only if protected
  • Reputation gameplay ‚Äî Track record of protection matters
  • Justifiable rebellion ‚Äî Secession has clear trigger
  • Multi-front pressure ‚Äî Overextended lieges collapse
  • Political theater ‚Äî Public accusations and defenses
  • No scripted enforcement ‚Äî All player-driven response
You get feudal breakdown without needing breach detection algorithms.
