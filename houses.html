What Are Houses?

A house is a chosen political entity ‚Äî an institution, a banner people rally under, a political body with legal standing and assets.

A house is what you build.

What Houses Are

A house is:

  • An institution ‚Äî A formal organization
  • A banner ‚Äî Something people rally under
  • A political body ‚Äî Has legal standing
  • Assets ‚Äî Owns property, storage, resources
  • Earned recognition ‚Äî Must be legitimized

You choose a house. You build a house. You defend a house.

What Houses Do Mechanically

Houses are where you give players meaningful persistence:

  • Shared property ‚Äî Hall, estate, buildings
  • Shared storage ‚Äî Chests, containers
  • Banners/colors ‚Äî Visual identity
  • Legal recognition ‚Äî Can petition court as a unit
  • Group action ‚Äî Ability to act collectively

Houses answer: "Who do you stand with now?"

Examples of Houses

  • House of the Crown ‚Äî Royal household
  • House Ashenvale ‚Äî Merchant collective
  • House Black Tide ‚Äî Criminal syndicate
  • House of the Flame ‚Äî Virtue zealots
  • House Ironcrown ‚Äî Military order

House Stark Example: Bloodline AND House (Both Layers)

To understand the distinction, consider House Stark from Game of Thrones. It functions as both ‚Äî but those are two different layers that overlap:

ü©∏ Stark Bloodline (Legitimacy Layer)

What it is:

  • The descendants of the Kings of Winter
  • A claim to the North by blood
  • A theory of legitimacy ("rule by lineage")

What it does:

  • Produces claimants
  • Shapes succession
  • Gives political weight to deaths and births
  • Cannot be appointed or revoked

What it owns: Nothing automatically. Bloodlines do not own land by default ‚Äî they justify why someone should hold it.

üè∞ House Stark (Organization Layer)

What it is:

  • A political/military organization
  • Includes: Stark blood members, Bannermen, Guards, Advisors, Bastards, Non-kin loyalists

What it does:

  • Holds land (Winterfell)
  • Commands soldiers
  • Manages alliances
  • Can be evicted, shattered, or exiled

What it owns: Winterfell, keeps, lands (by grant, conquest, or recognition)

Why This Separation Matters

If House Stark is defeated:

  • They can lose Winterfell
  • Their banners can be broken
  • Their people can scatter

But the Stark bloodline still exists:

  • Claimants remain
  • Rebellions can happen
  • "The North remembers"

That's good drama. Killing a Stark does not end House Stark. Evicting House Stark does not end the Stark bloodline. Destroying both requires sustained effort ‚Äî exactly the pacing you want in permadeath.

One Sentence to Remember: A bloodline explains why someone should rule; a house is how they actually exercise power.

Bloodlines: Inherited Identity

What Bloodlines Are

A bloodline is:

  • Ancestry ‚Äî Who your family was
  • Name ‚Äî Fixed surnames like Hawkwright, Rowan, Greymark
  • Reputation ‚Äî Social assumptions you inherit
  • Constraints ‚Äî Expectations and obligations
  • Memory ‚Äî History that predates you

You don't "join" a bloodline. You are born into it ‚Äî or not.

What Bloodlines Do Mechanically

Very little ‚Äî and that's intentional. Bloodlines should mostly provide:

  • Name + title formatting
  • Starting reputation
  • Initial relationships
  • Roleplay expectations

Bloodlines are constraints, not power. They answer: "Who were you before this story started?"

Why Bloodlines Matter on a Permadeath Shard

  • When someone dies, the bloodline loses a branch
  • Long-lived bloodlines feel legendary
  • Dying as "the last of your line" hurts (in a good way)
  • Rivalries persist even when individuals don't
  • Bloodlines give the world memory

  • House Black Tide ‚Äî Criminal syndicate
  • House of the Flame ‚Äî Virtue zealots

Why You Need BOTH (Not Just One)

If You Only Have Bloodlines:

  • Politics is static
  • Power calcifies
  • New players feel irrelevant
  • No room for social mobility

If You Only Have Houses:

  • Lineage feels meaningless
  • Deaths feel cheap
  • The world resets emotionally
  • No historical weight
Together: Bloodlines give history. Houses give movement. This is exactly how medieval politics worked.

How Bloodlines and Houses Interact

The relationship model that works best:

A Bloodline Member Can:

  • Join a house
  • Found a house
  • Betray their bloodline by joining a rival house
  • Elevate their bloodline through house success

A House Can:

  • Contain multiple bloodlines
  • Become dominated by one bloodline
  • Splinter along bloodline lines
  • Outlive any single bloodline

This creates organic drama without mechanics.

Common Examples (To Make It Click)

Example 1: Born Noble, Builds Power

  • Cedric Hawkwright (bloodline)
  • Founds House Ironcrown (house)
  • Becomes Marshal
  • Dies
  • House Ironcrown continues under new leadership

The bloodline provided legitimacy. The house provided structure.

Example 2: Lowborn Ascension

  • Tomas the Dockhand (no bloodline)
  • Joins House Ashenvale
  • Becomes its public face
  • Later crowned King
  • Bloodline irrelevant; house mattered

Houses allow social mobility that bloodlines prevent.

Example 3: Betrayal

  • Elara Rowan joins a merchant house
  • Family disowns her
  • Her bloodline reputation suffers
  • House gains prestige
  • Personal choice vs family obligation

This creates the tension that drives great RP.

Creating Player Houses

Players can create their own houses through a two-stage process:

Stage A: Unrecognized House (Low/No Mechanics)

Players can start a "house" socially any time:

  • "We are House Ashenvale now."
  • They wear matching colors
  • They claim a tavern table
  • They write a charter

Mechanically, it's just roleplay + maybe a cosmetic tag.

Stage B: Recognized House (Gets Real Perks)

To become official, they must be recognized by Britain (or later, any city).

How Recognition Works

Choose one of these methods:

  • Crown Charter ‚Äî The King issues a "Charter of House ___"
  • Great Council Recognition ‚Äî 3‚Äì5 high offices sign off
  • De Facto Recognition ‚Äî House proves itself over time

This is where mechanics engage.

What Recognized Houses Get

Reward Description
House Identity House name tag / title line, heraldry colors, registry entry
Legal Standing Can petition court as a house, demand trials/duels under house banner
Assets That Persist House Hall ownership survives member permadeath, leadership changes don't delete assets
Controlled Recruitment Can "adopt" new members via ceremony
Avoid giving: raw combat buffs, free gold, or item advantages. Houses should provide social and organizational power, not mechanical advantages.

How Players Create a House (Player-Facing Flow)

  1. Start a house socially
    • Pick name, colors, founder(s)
  2. Write a charter
    • 3‚Äì10 sentences: purpose, values, enemies, who can join
  3. Get recognized
    • Petition King, Regent, Magistrate, or Council of high offices
    • Must convince them through RP ‚Äî service, alliance, mutual benefit
    • Recognition is political, not automatic
  4. Hold a founding ceremony
    • Oaths + witnesses
  5. Now the house exists mechanically
    • Registry + tag + house hall claim

Preventing Common Problems

Risk 1: House Spam (20 houses in week 1)

Fix:

  • Recognition requirement + limit: each account can found 1 recognized house per season
  • Minimum active members (ex: 3‚Äì5)

Risk 2: Griefer Houses

Fix:

  • A house can be outlawed, stripped of recognition, or lose its hall claim
  • Not by "moderator punishment," but through legal / political process

Risk 3: Burnout from Leadership Duties

Fix:

  • Make leadership mostly symbolic
  • Give a "council" structure: House Head + 2 Deputies
  • Any of them can manage membership / hall permissions

What You Should NOT Do

  • ‚ùå Don't let bloodlines grant combat bonuses
  • ‚ùå Don't let houses grant raw stats
  • ‚ùå Don't force houses to map 1:1 with bloodlines
  • ‚ùå Don't let players create infinite bloodlines

Bloodlines should be curated. Houses should be earned.

Implementation Strategy for Season 1

If you're worried about complexity early:

  1. Launch with canon bloodlines only
  2. Allow unrecognized houses from day 1 (pure RP, no mechanics)
  3. After 2‚Äì3 weeks, enable recognized houses as a major "new era" moment

That gives Britain time to get spicy first, then player institutions emerge naturally.

Summary: The Design Health Check

Your system is healthy if:
  • Bloodlines feel heavy
  • Houses feel fragile
  • Offices feel dangerous
  • Death feels final
  • Memory outlives characters

You're designing a world where:

  • Identity is inherited (bloodlines)
  • Power is constructed (houses)
  • Legitimacy is contested (roles)
