The Mechanics of Tribute Flow

Gold flows where power commands.
Every tribute payment is a city's autonomy, converted to coin.

Core Principle

When a vassal city swears fealty to a liege, it agrees to pay tribute ‚Äî a percentage of its tax revenue automatically transferred to the liege's treasury.

This is NOT:

  • ‚ùå A manually paid gift
  • ‚ùå Negotiable after signing
  • ‚ùå Optional or voluntary once sworn
  • ‚ùå Avoidable without breaking fealty

This IS:

  • ‚úÖ Automatically siphoned from vassal treasury
  • ‚úÖ Calculated as % of collected tax revenue
  • ‚úÖ Logged publicly in Fealty Ledger
  • ‚úÖ Continuous until fealty is broken
  • ‚úÖ The primary economic lever of inter-city power
Design Victory: Tribute creates real budget strain without requiring manual collection.

How Tribute Calculation Works

Step 1: Tax Revenue Collection

Vassal city collects tax revenue from its citizens through the standard tax system:

  • Weekly property tax (~1-3% of property value)
  • Player shop vendor fees
  • Other city-specific revenue streams

Example: Yew collects 8,313 gold in tax revenue this week.

Step 2: Tribute Percentage Applied

The agreed tribute rate (from the fealty treaty) is applied to the gross tax revenue.

Tribute Formula

Tribute Amount = (Gross Tax Revenue) √ó (Tribute %)

Example:

Yew's tax revenue: 8,313 gold

Tribute rate to Britain: 15%

Tribute owed: 8,313 √ó 0.15 = 1,247 gold

Step 3: Automatic Siphon

The tribute amount is automatically transferred from vassal's treasury to liege's treasury:

  • No manual payment required
  • No option to withhold (unless breaking fealty)
  • Happens immediately after tax collection
  • Logged in both cities' treasury ledgers

Step 4: Net Revenue for Vassal

What remains goes to the vassal's treasury:

Net Revenue for Vassal

Yew's Net Revenue = 8,313 - 1,247 = 7,066 gold

This is what Yew's ruler has to work with for:

  • Official salaries
  • Guard wages
  • Property purchases
  • City infrastructure

Tribute Payment Timing

Weekly Cycle

Tribute flows on the same schedule as the tax collection cycle:

Day Event Treasury Impact
Sunday Tax collection runs Vassal treasury receives gross tax revenue
Sunday (moments later) Tribute calculated & transferred Gold automatically moves from vassal to liege
Sunday (after tribute) Net revenue remains Vassal can spend what's left

This happens every week, indefinitely, until fealty is broken.

What Happens If Vassal Can't Pay

Scenario: Insufficient Funds

What if the vassal's treasury doesn't have enough gold to cover tribute?

Option 1: Partial Payment (System Default)

  • System transfers whatever gold exists in vassal treasury
  • Shortfall is logged as "Partial Payment" in Fealty Ledger
  • Debt does NOT accumulate (no running balance)
  • Liege is notified of shortfall

Example:

Yew owes 1,247 gold but only has 500 gold in treasury.

System transfers 500 gold to Britain.

Ledger logs: "Tribute: 500 gold paid (Partial - 747 gold short)"

Option 2: Zero Payment (Empty Treasury)

  • No gold transferred (treasury is empty)
  • Logged as "Payment Failed - Insufficient Funds"
  • Liege receives notification
  • Political crisis potential

This is visible, embarrassing, and politically explosive.

Consequences of Nonpayment

The system doesn't mechanically punish nonpayment. Instead, the liege responds politically:

  • Declare breach of fealty (public accusation)
  • Withdraw protection guarantees
  • Demand explanation or compensation
  • Threaten military action
  • Support rival claimants to vassal's throne

See: Secession, Neutrality, and Rebellion

Multi-Vassal Treasury Flows

When a liege (usually the Capital) has multiple vassals, tribute flows from all of them:

Example: Britain with Three Vassals

Weekly Tribute Flow to Britain

Vassal City Tax Revenue Tribute % Gold to Britain
Yew 8,313 gold 15% 1,247 gold
Minoc 6,200 gold 10% 620 gold
Trinsic 11,500 gold 20% 2,300 gold
Total Tribute to Britain 4,167 gold/week

Result: Britain receives ~17,000 gold per month from vassals alone, on top of its own tax revenue.

Why This Creates Power Asymmetry

A Capital with vassals becomes dramatically wealthier:

  • More gold for official salaries ‚Üí Better governance
  • More gold for property acquisitions ‚Üí Economic dominance
  • More gold for bribes/rewards ‚Üí Political influence
  • More gold for mercenaries ‚Üí Military superiority
This is not a bug. Vassalage is supposed to enrich the liege at the vassal's expense.

Treasury Routing (Nested Vassals)

Can a Vassal Have Its Own Vassals?

Technically yes, but rare.

If Yew (vassal of Britain) gets Minoc to swear fealty to Yew, the flow looks like:

Nested Tribute Example

  1. Minoc collects 6,200 gold in taxes
  2. Minoc pays 10% (620 gold) to Yew (its liege)
  3. Yew collects 8,313 gold + 620 gold from Minoc = 8,933 gold total
  4. Yew pays 15% of its OWN revenue (8,313 √ó 0.15 = 1,247 gold) to Britain
  5. Britain receives 1,247 gold from Yew

Key: Yew does NOT pay Britain a cut of Minoc's tribute. Only its own tax revenue.

Design Note: Nested vassalage creates complex political hierarchies.
Most servers will discourage this to prevent bureaucratic overload.

Tribute vs. Other Revenue

Tribute is calculated ONLY on tax revenue, not other city income:

Income Source Subject to Tribute?
Property tax from residents ‚úÖ Yes
Vendor shop fees ‚úÖ Yes
Fines from courts/jails ‚ö†Ô∏è Server choice (define in treaty)
Donations from players ‚ùå No (not tax revenue)
Property sales to other players ‚ùå No (one-time transaction)
Loot from PvP/raids ‚ùå No (not systematic revenue)

Transparency prevents disputes: Only revenue logged in Tax Ledger counts for tribute calculation.

Adjusting Tribute Rates

Can Tribute % Change?

Yes, but NOT unilaterally.

Tribute rates are part of the fealty treaty. To change them:

  1. Liege proposes new rate (usually higher)
  2. Vassal can accept, negotiate, or refuse
  3. If agreed, treaty is amended and logged
  4. New rate takes effect immediately

If vassal refuses:

  • Liege cannot force the change mechanically
  • Liege may threaten withdrawal of protection
  • Liege may declare breach and terminate fealty
  • Vassal may choose to break fealty preemptively

Scenario: Tribute Increase Demand

Political Crisis Example

1485, May: Britain demands Yew increase tribute from 15% to 25%.

Yew's Options:

  • Accept: Budget collapses, residents angry, ruler unpopular
  • Negotiate: Offer 18% as compromise
  • Refuse: Britain may withdraw protection, back rival claimant
  • Break fealty: Formal secession, become independent (and vulnerable)

This is political gameplay gold.

When Tribute Stops

Tribute only flows while fealty is active. It stops when:

Event Tribute Effect
Vassal breaks fealty Stops immediately, no retroactive payment
Liege breaks fealty Stops immediately
Vassal deactivates (population drops below threshold) Suspends until reactivation
Liege loses Capital status ‚ö†Ô∏è Does NOT automatically stop (fealty can exist without Capital status)
Treaty expires (if time-limited) Stops on expiration date

Visibility & Logging

Every tribute payment is logged publicly in the Fealty Ledger:

What Gets Logged

  • Date/time of payment
  • Amount transferred
  • Percentage of vassal's tax revenue
  • Vassal's total tax revenue (for transparency)
  • Payment status: Full, Partial, or Failed

Why This Matters

Public logging creates:

  • Accountability: Vassal can't hide tax revenue from liege
  • Intelligence: Other cities can see who pays whom
  • Pressure: Failed payments are embarrassing and visible
  • Proof: Evidence for breach accusations

Economic Impact on Vassals

Budget Strain

Tribute creates immediate financial pressure on vassals:

  • Lower budget for official salaries ‚Üí Harder to attract talent
  • Less gold for property acquisition ‚Üí Economic stagnation
  • Reduced emergency funds ‚Üí Vulnerable to crises
  • Resentment among residents ‚Üí "We're paying taxes to Britain!"

Internal Political Friction

Tribute creates factional conflict within vassal cities:

  • Loyalists: "We need Britain's protection"
  • Rebels: "We should break fealty and keep our gold"
  • Merchants: "Can we negotiate better trade terms?"
  • Ruler: Caught in the middle, trying to balance budget and politics
This internal conflict is not a bug. It's the point.

Common Questions

Q: Can I refuse to pay tribute without breaking fealty?

A: No. Tribute is automatic. If you want to stop paying, you must formally break fealty.

Q: What if the liege demands an unreasonable rate (50%+)?

A: You can refuse to sign the treaty. If already sworn, you can renegotiate or break fealty. Unsustainable rates pressure both sides to be realistic.

Q: Does tribute count as an expense I can deduct from my city budget?

A: No. Tribute is calculated on gross tax revenue before any expenses. It comes off the top.

Q: Can I hide tax revenue to avoid paying full tribute?

A: No. Tax revenue is automatically calculated and logged. There's no way to "hide" it.

Q: If I break fealty, do I owe back-tribute?

A: No. Tribute only flows while fealty is active. Past weeks that were paid stay paid. Future weeks after breaking are not owed.

Q: What if my city is attacked and I need emergency funds, but tribute drains my treasury?

A: This is the political tension. You can beg the liege for financial aid, negotiate tribute reduction, or break fealty and face the consequences.

Design Intent

Tribute mechanics create:

  • Real economic hierarchy ‚Äî Lieges get richer, vassals get poorer
  • Automatic pressure ‚Äî No need for manual collection
  • Internal conflict ‚Äî Vassals debate whether submission is worth it
  • Political crises ‚Äî Shortfalls trigger confrontations
  • Transparency ‚Äî Everyone can see the money flow
You get feudal economics without needing a tax collector role.
