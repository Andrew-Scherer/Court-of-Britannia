The Contract Between Cities

Fealty is not ownership. It is leverage.
Britain does not rule other cities. It makes their choices expensive.

Core Principle

Fealty is a political contract between two city-states where one (the vassal) acknowledges the supremacy of another (the liege) in exchange for protection, recognition, or privilege.

Fealty is NOT:

  • ‚ùå Ownership or direct rule
  • ‚ùå Control over internal politics
  • ‚ùå Command of guards or officials
  • ‚ùå Permanent or irrevocable

Fealty IS:

  • ‚úÖ A voluntary agreement (to enter)
  • ‚úÖ Politically expensive (to leave)
  • ‚úÖ Publicly visible
  • ‚úÖ Structurally asymmetric
  • ‚úÖ Recognition-based, not force-based

Recognition vs. Fealty

These are separate but related concepts:

Concept Definition Mechanical Effect
Recognition Ideological alignment with a capital's legitimacy theory Social/narrative only (see Capital Recognition)
Fealty Political contract with obligations and benefits Tribute payments, protection guarantees, treaty enforcement

A city can:

  • Recognize a capital ideologically but not swear fealty (support without submission)
  • Swear fealty without recognition (pragmatic submission)
  • Do both (full alignment)
  • Do neither (neutrality or opposition)

The Fealty Relationship

The Two Parties

Liege City (Superior)

Usually: The Capital (but not always)

Can demand:

  • Tribute (percentage of vassal's tax revenue)
  • Recognition of warrants and decrees
  • Military cooperation
  • Symbolic submission

Must offer:

  • Protection promises
  • Recognition of vassal's ruler
  • Economic or political privileges

Vassal City (Subordinate)

Must provide:

  • Agreed tribute payments
  • Public acknowledgment of fealty
  • Cooperation with liege's policies

Receives:

  • Military protection
  • Legitimacy for their ruler
  • Possible economic benefits

Retains:

  • Internal governance autonomy
  • Local taxation authority
  • Own courts and laws
  • Own guard command

What the Liege Can Demand

1. Tax Tribute (Primary Lever)

The liege may demand a percentage of the vassal's tax revenue.

Tribute Mechanics

  • Automatically siphoned from vassal treasury
  • Publicly logged in Fealty Ledger
  • Adjustable by treaty, not by fiat
  • Stops if vassal deactivates (insufficient population)

Example:

Yew agrees to remit 15% of all city tax revenue to Britain.

This creates:

  • Immediate budget strain on Yew
  • Resentment among Yew residents
  • Pressure on Yew's ruler
  • Enrichment of Britain's treasury

See: Inter-City Tithes & Treasury Routing for full mechanics

2. Warrant Recognition (Soft Power)

The vassal may be required to:

  • Recognize arrest warrants issued by the liege
  • Allow liege guards to operate in vassal territory
  • Extradite criminals wanted by the liege
  • Honor exile decrees from the liege

This is recognition, not command. The vassal's guards still answer to their own city ‚Äî but cooperation is expected.

3. Symbolic Submission (Visibility)

Optional but powerful political theater:

  • Flying the liege's banner alongside city banner
  • Public ceremonies recognizing fealty
  • Herald announcements of subordinate status
  • Ruler's title prefixed with "Under [Liege City]"

Purely political, but extremely visible and highly combustible.

What the Liege Must Offer

1. Protection Guarantee

The liege pledges:

  • Military response to invasion or rebellion
  • Support during internal crises
  • Guard assistance when requested
Critical: Protection is not automatic. It is a promise.
Failure to respond is a political event, not a system failure.

See: Protection Promises & Failure

2. Political Recognition

The liege recognizes the vassal's ruler as legitimate, which:

  • Endorses their authority publicly
  • Legitimizes controversial leadership
  • Deters rival claimants
  • Turns the liege into a kingmaker

This makes succession in vassal cities political at the realm level.

3. Economic Privilege (Optional)

Possible concessions the liege may grant:

  • Reduced tribute rate
  • Trade protections or monopolies
  • Access to Capital-only markets
  • Preferential treatment in disputes

Merchants care deeply about this. Cities can fracture internally over economic alignment.

Autonomy Is Preserved

A vassal city retains complete internal sovereignty:

Authority Vassal Retains?
Appointment of local officials ‚úÖ Yes
Setting local tax rates ‚úÖ Yes (but pays tribute on collected taxes)
Running local courts ‚úÖ Yes
Command of local guards ‚úÖ Yes
Jailing prisoners ‚úÖ Yes
Property ownership rules ‚úÖ Yes
Internal succession ‚ö†Ô∏è Yes, but liege recognition matters

The liege does not micromanage. It extracts.

Design Victory: This prevents administrative overload, empire micromanagement, and Britain becoming a global GM role.

Entering Fealty

Fealty is always voluntary to enter (though refusal may have consequences).

The Proposal Process

  1. Liege proposes fealty terms ‚Äî Tribute %, protection promises, privileges
  2. Proposal delivered to vassal ruler ‚Äî Via emissary, decree, or direct negotiation
  3. Vassal reviews terms ‚Äî May negotiate, accept, or refuse
  4. If agreed, treaty is signed ‚Äî Logged in Fealty Ledger
  5. Public announcement fires ‚Äî Realm-wide visibility
  6. Tribute begins immediately ‚Äî Systems engage

Negotiation Window

After proposal, vassal has 72 hours to respond.

If no response:

  • Proposal expires automatically
  • No fealty established
  • Liege may interpret silence as refusal

Why Cities Swear Fealty

Not all submission is forced. Cities may voluntarily swear fealty because:

  • Protection: They face external threats (raiders, rival cities)
  • Legitimacy: New ruler needs Capital recognition
  • Economics: Access to trade privileges outweighs tribute cost
  • Weakness: Cannot survive independently
  • Ideology: Genuinely believe in liege's supremacy

Leaving Fealty (Secession)

Fealty can be broken, but not without consequence.

Methods of Exit

Method Process Consequence
Formal Withdrawal Vassal announces intent to break fealty Liege may demand reasons, negotiate retention, or accept loss
Nonpayment Vassal stops paying tribute Liege declares breach, may withdraw protection or declare rebellion
Defiance Vassal publicly rejects liege authority Immediate hostility, liege may rally other vassals against them
Liege Collapses Liege loses Capital status or deactivates Vassals automatically released, become neutral

See: Secession, Neutrality, and Rebellion

Consequences of Breaking Fealty

  • Protection withdraws immediately
  • Tribute stops flowing
  • Economic privileges revoked
  • Liege may back rival claimants
  • Other vassals may be pressured to shun former vassal
  • Liege may sponsor coups or invasions
No auto-war. Breaking fealty does not trigger mechanical combat.
Consequences are political, not scripted.

Multiple Vassals (The Realm)

A liege city (usually the Capital) may have multiple vassals simultaneously.

Example Realm Structure

Britain (Liege / Capital)

Vassals:

  • Yew ‚Äî Pays 15% tribute, receives protection
  • Minoc ‚Äî Pays 10% tribute, receives trade privilege
  • Trinsic ‚Äî Pays 20% tribute (contested ruler needs recognition)

Neutral Cities:

  • Vesper ‚Äî Refuses fealty, independent

Result: Britain receives steady tribute from three cities while Vesper remains vulnerable but free.

Inter-Vassal Politics

Vassals may:

  • Compete for lower tribute rates
  • Form coalitions to pressure the liege
  • Spy on each other
  • Coordinate mass withdrawal
  • Backstab each other to gain favor

The liege plays vassals against each other. This is not a bug.

Breach of Contract

Either party can violate the fealty agreement.

Vassal Breach

Examples:

  • Stops paying tribute
  • Refuses to honor liege's warrants
  • Harbors liege's enemies
  • Attacks liege's other vassals

Liege Response Options:

  • Declare breach publicly
  • Withdraw protection
  • Demand increased tribute as penalty
  • Back rival claimant to vassal's throne
  • Rally other vassals to punish the breach

Liege Breach

Examples:

  • Fails to provide promised protection
  • Demands tribute beyond agreed rate
  • Backs coup against vassal's ruler
  • Revokes privileges without cause

Vassal Response Options:

  • Declare breach publicly (narrative weapon)
  • Withhold tribute
  • Formal withdrawal from fealty
  • Seek new liege
  • Coordinate with other vassals to pressure liege
No referee. No auto-enforcement. Only political consequences.

Fealty Without the Capital

Fealty does not require Capital status. Any Active city can propose fealty to another.

Scenario: Non-Capital Liege

Yew (not the Capital) convinces Minoc to swear fealty:

  • Minoc pays tribute to Yew, not Britain
  • Yew offers protection to Minoc
  • Britain may view this as defiance or alliance-building

Result: Power blocs form outside Capital control. This is realm-level politics.

Common Questions

Q: Can a vassal have its own vassals?

A: Technically yes, but rarely practical. A vassal paying tribute upward while collecting tribute downward creates complex chains. Most systems discourage this.

Q: Can I refuse a fealty proposal without consequences?

A: You can refuse mechanically. But the liege may respond politically ‚Äî withdrawal of protection, economic pressure, backing your rivals.

Q: What if the Capital demands impossible tribute (50%+)?

A: The vassal can refuse or negotiate. If tribute is unsustainable, the vassal's economy collapses, residents flee, and the city may deactivate. This pressures both sides to be realistic.

Q: Can I be forced into fealty?

A: Not mechanically. But you can be coerced through military threat, economic blockade, or political isolation.

Q: If I swear fealty, can the liege appoint my officials?

A: No. You retain full internal governance. They can pressure, threaten, or manipulate ‚Äî but not directly appoint.

Q: What happens if both the liege and vassal deactivate?

A: Fealty treaty suspends. If both reactivate later, treaty resumes unless formally revoked.

Design Intent

Fealty creates:

  • Asymmetric relationships without direct rule
  • Economic interdependence that creates pressure
  • Conditional protection that can fail
  • Visible submission that creates resentment
  • Breakable contracts that lead to conflict
You get empire gameplay without empire mechanics.
