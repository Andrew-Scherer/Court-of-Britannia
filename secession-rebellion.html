⚔️ Secession, Neutrality, and Rebellion

Breaking the Chains of Fealty

Every vassal dreams of freedom.
Every liege fears loss of control.
This tension is the engine of conflict.

Core Principle

Fealty is voluntary to enter but politically costly to leave. A vassal city can break fealty at any time, but the consequences are immediate, public, and severe.

Breaking fealty is NOT:

  • ❌ Mechanically blocked or prevented
  • ❌ Automatically punished by the system
  • ❌ Reversible without negotiation
  • ❌ Hidden or secretive

Breaking fealty IS:

  • ✅ Always mechanically possible
  • ✅ Publicly logged and announced
  • ✅ Politically explosive
  • ✅ A catalyst for realm-level conflict
  • ✅ The vassal's ultimate leverage
Design Victory: The threat of secession creates negotiating power without needing admin intervention.

Methods of Breaking Fealty

1. Formal Withdrawal (Diplomatic)

The Polite Exit

Process:

  1. Vassal announces formal intent to withdraw from fealty
  2. Reason stated (optional but politically wise)
  3. Notice period offered (e.g., 30 days, per treaty)
  4. Logged in Fealty Ledger
  5. Tribute stops at end of notice period

Liege's Response Options:

  • Accept gracefully (preserve future relationship)
  • Negotiate concessions to retain vassal
  • Demand compensation for lost tribute
  • Withdraw protection immediately
  • Declare vassal in rebellion (hostile interpretation)

When to use: Vassal has leverage, wants to minimize hostility, or seeks renegotiation.

2. Nonpayment (Passive Resistance)

The Silent Rebellion

Process:

  1. Vassal stops paying tribute (treasury empty or withheld)
  2. Nonpayment logged publicly
  3. Liege notified of shortfall
  4. Liege interprets as breach or incompetence

Liege's Response Options:

  • Demand explanation
  • Declare breach of fealty
  • Terminate fealty and withdraw protection
  • Offer financial aid (if strategic)
  • Sponsor coup against vassal's ruler

When to use: Vassal can't afford tribute, wants plausible deniability, or is testing liege's response.

See: Inter-City Tithes for tribute payment mechanics

3. Open Defiance (Revolutionary)

The Declaration of Independence

Process:

  1. Vassal publicly denounces liege's authority
  2. "We reject Britain's tyranny and declare ourselves free!"
  3. Immediate cessation of tribute
  4. Often coupled with revocation of Capital recognition
  5. Realm-wide announcement

Liege's Response Options:

  • Declare vassal in rebellion (war footing)
  • Rally other vassals to pressure or attack rebel
  • Back rival claimant to rebel's throne
  • Impose economic sanctions or blockade
  • Military invasion (if capable)

When to use: Vassal has military strength, ideological conviction, or allied support. Point of no return.

4. Liege-Initiated Termination

The Rejection

Process:

  1. Liege terminates fealty agreement (rare but possible)
  2. Reason stated (e.g., "Yew has proven unreliable")
  3. Tribute stops immediately
  4. Protection guarantees end

Why a liege might do this:

  • Vassal more trouble than valuable
  • Political optics ("We don't tolerate betrayal")
  • Vassal deactivated or collapsed economically
  • Strategic pivot to different alliance

When to use: Liege wants to cut losses or punish publicly.

Immediate Consequences of Secession

Automatic System Effects

Effect Happens Immediately
Tribute stops ✅ Yes — No more gold flows to liege
Treaty voided ✅ Yes — Logged as "Terminated" in Fealty Ledger
Protection guarantees end ✅ Yes — Liege has no obligation to defend
Recognition remains ⚠️ Optional — Vassal may keep or revoke Capital recognition
War declaration ❌ No — Liege must choose to respond hostilely
Critical: Secession does not auto-trigger war or sanctions.
Those are political choices the liege must make.

Political Consequences

What happens next depends on how the liege responds:

Liege Response Matrix

Response Intent Vassal Impact
Accept Peacefully Preserve goodwill, avoid conflict Vassal becomes neutral, safe
Negotiate Retention Offer better terms to keep vassal Possible compromise, fealty continues
Declare Breach Public condemnation, reputation damage Vassal labeled untrustworthy
Withdraw Protection Leave vassal vulnerable Exposed to external threats
Economic Sanctions Pressure other cities to shun rebel Trade disruption, isolation
Back Rival Claimant Sponsor coup to replace ruler Internal succession crisis
Military Invasion Forceful reconquest or punishment War, potential occupation

Post-Secession Status Options

1. Independence (Neutrality)

Vassal becomes a neutral, sovereign city:

  • No tribute obligations
  • No protection guarantees
  • Full budget control
  • Vulnerable to aggression
  • Free to negotiate new treaties

Risk: Former liege may sponsor rival claimants, economic pressure, or military threats.

2. Seek New Liege

Vassal immediately swears fealty to a different city:

  • Betrayal optics if done quickly
  • New protection guarantees
  • New tribute obligations (possibly better terms)
  • Former liege views as hostile act

Example: Yew Switches Lieges

1485, May 1: Yew breaks fealty with Britain

1485, May 3: Yew swears fealty to Vesper (rival power)

Result:

  • Britain views this as ultimate betrayal
  • Yew's tribute now flows to Vesper
  • Vesper gains economic/political advantage
  • Britain likely backs coup or invasion of Yew
  • Realm-level power shift begins

3. Form Counter-Alliance

Vassal coordinates with other vassals to break fealty simultaneously:

  • Mass secession event
  • Coalition of former vassals
  • Collective defense pact
  • Liege's power collapses

Example: The Great Rebellion of 1486

Coordination: Yew, Minoc, and Trinsic secretly plan simultaneous secession

Execution:

  1. All three break fealty on same day
  2. Immediate mutual defense pact signed
  3. Public declaration: "Britain's tyranny ends today"
  4. Britain loses 4,000+ gold/week in tribute

Result:

  • Britain's budget collapses overnight
  • Can't afford to fight all three simultaneously
  • Forced to negotiate or watch realm fracture
  • Server-defining political event

Why Vassals Secede

Common motivations for breaking fealty:

Reason Description
Economic Burden Tribute unsustainable, city budget collapsing
Protection Failure Liege failed to defend when attacked (see Protection Promises)
Ideological Shift New ruler rejects previous alignment
Opportunism Liege weakened, vassal sees chance to escape
External Offer Rival power offers better terms
Internal Pressure Residents demand independence
Tribute Increase Liege demands higher %, vassal refuses

Why Lieges Let Vassals Go

Sometimes lieges choose NOT to fight secession:

  • Cost-benefit: War more expensive than tribute gained
  • Strategic focus: Prioritize other conflicts
  • Weakness: Can't afford military response
  • Optics: Graceful release looks stronger than failed reconquest
  • Diplomacy: Preserve future alliance potential
A smart liege knows when to let go. A weak one fights every loss and loses everything.

Rebellion vs. Secession

What's the Difference?

Term Definition Connotation
Secession Formal withdrawal from fealty Neutral or legitimate (depending on perspective)
Rebellion Liege's term for secession they consider illegitimate Hostile, implies criminality

Key Insight: "Rebellion" is a label applied by the liege, not a mechanical state.

When a vassal breaks fealty:

  • Vassal perspective: "We have seceded"
  • Liege perspective: "They are in rebellion"
  • System logs it as: "Fealty Terminated"

Neutrality

Choosing to Stay Free

A city can choose never to swear fealty:

  • Full autonomy
  • No tribute obligations
  • No guaranteed protection
  • Vulnerable but independent

Pressure to swear fealty comes from:

  • Military threats from stronger cities
  • Economic isolation
  • Ideological expectation (e.g., "All cities should recognize Britain")
  • Internal factions wanting protection
Neutrality is difficult to maintain.
Powerful cities will pressure neutrals to pick a side.

Violence & War

Does Secession Cause War?

Not automatically. War is a political choice.

When a vassal secedes, the liege may:

  • Declare war (formal announcement)
  • Launch military action (guards, mercenaries, player armies)
  • Siege vassal city (if system supports it)
  • Sponsor coups (back rival claimant)
  • Raid and harass (economic warfare)

But all of this is player-driven, not scripted.

The system enables conflict. It does not mandate it.

Reconciliation

Can a Seceded Vassal Return?

Yes, but it requires new treaty negotiation.

If Yew breaks fealty then wants to return:

  1. Yew proposes new fealty terms
  2. Britain can accept, negotiate, or refuse
  3. Britain may demand:
    • Higher tribute rate as punishment
    • Public apology or symbolic submission
    • Compensation for lost tribute
    • Hostages or guarantees
  4. If agreed, new treaty signed
  5. Tribute resumes

Political cost: Yew's reputation damaged ("They crawled back"). Future secession threats less credible.

Multi-Vassal Dynamics

The Domino Effect

When one vassal breaks fealty, others watch closely:

  • If liege responds weakly: Other vassals feel emboldened to secede
  • If liege responds brutally: Other vassals fear seceding, but resentment grows
  • If liege negotiates: Other vassals demand better terms too

Example: The Cascade

1486, March: Yew breaks fealty, Britain does nothing

1486, April: Minoc breaks fealty, citing Britain's weakness

1486, May: Trinsic threatens to break unless tribute reduced

Result: Britain's realm collapses due to initial inaction.

Real-World Design Goals

Secession mechanics create:

  • Vassal leverage: Threat of leaving pressures lieges to be fair
  • Realm instability: Vassals can coordinate revolts
  • Political history: Famous secessions become server lore
  • Succession crises: New rulers may reject old fealty
  • Alliance gameplay: Former vassals seek new protectors
You get revolutionary gameplay without needing "declare war" buttons.

Common Questions

Q: Can I break fealty at any time?

A: Yes. Mechanically, you can always break fealty. Politically, there may be severe consequences.

Q: If I break fealty, can the liege force me back?

A: Not mechanically. They can pressure you politically, militarily, or economically, but you cannot be forced into fealty.

Q: Do I owe back-tribute if I secede?

A: No. Tribute stops immediately upon secession. Past payments are settled.

Q: What if my city deactivates while under fealty?

A: Fealty suspends automatically. Tribute stops. If you reactivate, fealty can resume or be renegotiated.

Q: Can I break fealty to avoid an imminent attack?

A: Yes, but it won't stop the attack. Breaking fealty removes your protection guarantee, making you more vulnerable, not less.

Q: What if all vassals break fealty simultaneously?

A: The liege loses all tribute income and political power. This is a catastrophic event for the liege and a realm-defining moment.

Design Intent

Secession mechanics create:

  • Asymmetric power balance — Vassals are weaker but have exit option
  • Political instability — Realms can collapse organically
  • Negotiation leverage — Threat of leaving forces compromise
  • Historical events — Secessions become server legends
  • Emergent conflict — No scripted wars, all player-driven
You get revolution gameplay without needing a revolution system.
