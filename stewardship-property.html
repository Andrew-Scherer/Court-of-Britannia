⚖️ Stewardship & Property

One Property. One Person. Living Land.

Core Principle

Land belongs to people.
Institutions inhabit land.
Power ends where life ends.

Every property in the world is anchored to a single living character.

Guilds, cities, and institutions exist through people, not abstractions.

This system ensures:

  • Permadeath always matters
  • Land never becomes immortal
  • Power always carries personal risk
  • The map remains dynamic and contested

The One Property Rule

Every Character May Own Exactly One Property

This applies to all property types, including:

  • City homes
  • Historic sites
  • Keeps, forts, monasteries
  • Guild halls
  • Any claimable structure

There are no exceptions.

If you own a property, you cannot own another.

Property Ownership

Property ownership is always personal.

  • Properties are purchased by individuals (e.g. through auction)
  • Ownership is tied to a single character
  • Property never belongs directly to a guild
Guilds do not "own" land.
They anchor themselves to land.

Stewardship

What Is a Steward?

A Property Steward is the individual who binds a property to a guild.

The steward:

  • Is the legal anchor of the property
  • Bears the risk of death, defection, or disappearance
  • Has the permission of the guildstone (and guild leader) to make a property a legitimate guild site
A steward is not automatically made the guild leader.

They can also be the guild leader, but not automatically.

Guild Property (Guild-Anchored Land)

A property becomes a guild property when a guildstone is placed on it.

Placing a guildstone:

  • Anchors the property to that guild
  • Designates the property as a guild site
  • Binds the steward's personal property claim to the guild

Once bound:

  • The steward may not own any other property
  • The property cannot be reclaimed as personal housing unless the guildstone is removed
  • The steward burns their personal claim to give the guild a home

Guild Leader vs Property Steward

These are separate roles.

Guild Leader

  • Directs the guild
  • Handles politics, war, diplomacy
  • May change without affecting land ownership

Property Steward

  • Anchors the land
  • Bears legal and existential risk
  • Creates continuity and vulnerability

They may be the same person, but they do not have to be.

This separation allows:

  • Leadership to rotate safely
  • Institutions to endure change
  • Risk without brittleness

Co-Ownership & Access

Properties may have co-owners or residents.

Co-ownership allows:

  • Shared access
  • Storage and permissions
  • Social and family housing
  • Guild member residency
However:

Co-owners do not anchor land.
Only the Steward does.

Co-ownership never replaces stewardship.

Access can be shared.
Responsibility cannot.

Steward Death

When a steward dies (permadeath):

  • The property enters Interregnum
  • The guildstone remains
  • The property continues to exist
  • The guild remains present but unstabilized

This creates a succession window.

Steward Departure (Defection or Resignation)

If a steward leaves the guild voluntarily:

  • The guildstone remains active
  • The property enters Interregnum
  • The departing steward has no claim to the property
  • Their personal property slot is freed

They may:

  • Join another guild
  • Purchase another property
  • Continue playing normally

They cannot:

  • Keep the property
  • Transfer it
  • Destroy the guild's claim
This is treated as a soft death of stewardship.

Interregnum

Interregnum is a temporary state where a property lacks a steward.

During Interregnum

The guildstone remains active:

  • Residents retain access
  • Normal guild activity continues
  • Crown standing remains unchanged initially

Limitations during Interregnum:

  • No major upgrades or expansions
  • No ownership transfers
  • No re-anchoring or sale actions

Interregnum Is Meant To Be

  • A minor inconvenience
  • A succession prompt
  • A political opportunity

Not an apocalypse.

Resolving Interregnum

Any co-owner who is also a guild member may become steward if they:

  • Are alive
  • Own no other property
  • Bind themselves at the guildstone

Co-Owner Priority Window

Co-owner guild members have first right of stewardship during the initial Interregnum period (48-hour window).

After 48 hours, stewardship opens to any guild member meeting the requirements.

Once bound:

  • Interregnum ends immediately
  • Full functionality is restored
  • The property is stabilized

Failure to Resolve Interregnum

If Interregnum is not resolved:

After 24 Hours

  • Global bank access is suspended
  • Upkeep penalties are doubled

After 48 Hours

  • Upkeep penalties are tripled
  • Decorations begin to decay

Final Decay

After 7 days without a Steward, the guildstone decays.

  • The property is relinquished
  • The site returns to public auction
This ensures land never stagnates indefinitely.

Why Stewardship Exists

Design Reason

Guilds do not die.
People do.

If land belonged directly to guilds:

  • Death would lose meaning
  • Institutions would become immortal
  • Maps would ossify
  • Power would freeze

Stewardship ensures:

  • Every property is mortal
  • Every institution rests on flesh
  • Every claim can be challenged

Technical Reason

Property systems are simpler, safer, and more predictable when:

  • Ownership always resolves to a character
  • Death, taxes, decay, and auctions follow one model

The steward model avoids:

  • Dual ownership logic
  • Inheritance edge cases
  • Guild-death paradoxes

Soft Immortality (What This Prevents)

If guild leader = land owner:

  • Leaders never rotate
  • Internal politics freeze
  • Assassination becomes the only lever

By separating roles:

  • Leadership can change without risking land
  • Land can change without collapsing leadership
  • Stability and volatility coexist
Risk is localized, not totalized.

Final Philosophy

Land should outlive leaders — but not people.

This system:

  • Rewards commitment
  • Punishes hoarding
  • Encourages succession
  • Keeps history alive
  • Keeps the map moving
No spreadsheets.
No abstractions.
No immortal empires.

Just people, land, and consequences.
