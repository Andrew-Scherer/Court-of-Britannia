What Happens When the King Dies

The Crown transfers only at coronation.
Until then, the realm is governed by a Regent, and the Heir holds only a claim.

Main Idea

Upon the King's death, the Crown is vacant. The Regent governs in trust. Named Heirs hold official claims in order of precedence. Claimants may assert contested claims through force or alliance. The Crown transfers only at coronation, where any eligible contender‚ÄîHeir, Regent, or Claimant‚Äîmay be crowned. Legitimacy is determined not by mechanics, but by who recognizes the outcome.

Core Principle: Vacant Crown, Delegated Authority

Crisis-first, story-forward model

At the moment the King dies:

The Succession Crisis Begins

  • The Crown is vacant ‚Äî No one is King
  • The Heir holds a claim, not authority ‚Äî Symbol and blood right, but no powers
  • The Regent holds King powers temporarily ‚Äî Full governance "in trust"
  • Claimants may contest the succession ‚Äî Alternative claims through bloodline, force, or alliance
  • Coronation = actual transfer of Crown authority ‚Äî The moment everything changes

Authority Layer (Who holds power before coronation)

Power Structure During Vacancy

  • Regent holds all governing authority ‚Äî Full King powers, temporarily delegated
  • Heirs hold symbolic legitimacy only ‚Äî Official claim, but no executive power
  • Claimants hold nothing unless they seize it ‚Äî Contested legitimacy, no authority

This preserves the clean triangle:

Heir = legitimacy without power
Regent = power without claim
Claimant = claim without power

That triangle is the engine of drama.

Eligibility Layer (Who is allowed to be crowned)

At the King's death, the following are eligible for coronation:

Eligibility for Coronation

  • Named Heirs (Primary / Secondary / Tertiary)
    ‚Üí Official, ordered eligibility
  • Claimants
    ‚Üí Unofficial, contested eligibility
  • The Regent
    ‚Üí Procedural eligibility through custody of the state

So the coronation is not about transfer ‚Äî it is about selection.

The Key Distinction

No one has King powers until coronation.

The Regent holds sovereign authority, but not the Crown.

Critical Rule:
The Crown is not inherited automatically. It is claimed, contested, and finally transferred at coronation.

Step-by-Step: What Happens When a King Dies

When a King dies and both an Heir and Regent exist:

Step 1: The Crown Becomes Vacant Immediately

No one is King yet.

The throne is empty. All King-specific powers are suspended. The realm enters a state of uncertainty.

Step 2: The Heir Becomes Heir Presumptive, Not King

They hold:

  • Claim ‚Äî Legitimacy based on appointment or bloodline
  • Symbol ‚Äî They represent the future
  • Blood right ‚Äî Social and legal standing

But no King powers.

The Heir cannot command guards, issue decrees, or wield royal authority. They are vulnerable, watched, and often hunted.

‚Üí See Heir Presumptive for complete details on the Heir role

Step 3: The Regent Assumes Full King Authority Temporarily

They may:

  • Command guards
  • Issue decrees
  • Govern the realm

All stamped "REGENT AUTHORITY".

The Regent is not King, but they hold all the power. This creates the central tension of succession.

‚Üí See The Regent for complete details on Regent powers and limitations

Step 4: The Coronation Is the Transfer Point

At coronation:

  • Whoever is crowned becomes King ‚Äî Not before
  • King powers transfer at that moment ‚Äî Full sovereignty begins
  • Regent authority dissolves instantly ‚Äî Their temporary power ends

This makes the coronation the single most important event in the realm.

Succession is not a rule ‚Äî it's a test.

Heirs test legitimacy
Regents test restraint
Claimants test strength

The Three-Way Dynamic: Heir vs Regent vs Claimant

The Heir Presumptive

Legitimacy Without Power

What they have:

  • Official claim to the throne
  • Appointed by previous King OR bloodline succession
  • Public recognition
  • Legal standing

What they lack:

  • King powers (cannot command guards, issue decrees)
  • Protection (vulnerable to assassination)
  • Certainty (coronation is not guaranteed)

Their goal: Survive until coronation

‚Üí Full details: Heir Presumptive

The Regent

Power Without Legitimacy

What they have:

  • All King powers (temporarily)
  • Command of guards
  • Ability to issue decrees
  • Control over the coronation process

What they lack:

  • The Crown itself
  • Permanent legitimacy
  • Long-term stability

Their temptation: Crown themselves instead of the Heir

‚Üí Full details: The Regent

Claimants

Competing Claims

What they have:

  • Alternative claim to the throne (bloodline, conquest, right)
  • Potential supporters
  • Ability to contest the succession

What they lack:

  • Official recognition (unlike the Heir)
  • Legal standing (their claim is disputed)
  • Power (unless they seize it)

Their goal: Disrupt the coronation and press their own claim

The Coronation Timer: Pressure Cooker

The moment the King dies and a Regent exists, a coronation window begins (typically 3-7 real days).

Everyone knows:
  • There will be a coronation
  • Someone will be crowned
  • Or blood will be spilled

What Can Happen During the Window

Actor Possible Actions
Heir Secure protection, build alliances, survive assassination attempts
Regent Govern, delay coronation (once), advance it (risky), or crown themselves
Claimants Press claims, gather support, challenge legitimacy, plot coups
Marshal Choose sides, protect Heir or back Regent, remain neutral
Nobles Support various factions, negotiate deals, position for power

Why This Design Works

It Preserves:

  • Succession tension ‚Äî Nothing is automatic
  • Regent temptation ‚Äî They hold all the cards
  • Heir vulnerability ‚Äî Claim without power is dangerous
  • Public legitimacy gameplay ‚Äî How people react matters

It Creates Clean Drama:

  • Until coronation, everything is provisional ‚Äî No certainty
  • Every delay is suspicious ‚Äî What is the Regent waiting for?
  • Every protection detail matters ‚Äî The Heir can die
  • Every decree can be challenged later ‚Äî "REGENT AUTHORITY" is temporary

It Avoids Edge-Case Confusion:

  • No "King but not really King" ‚Äî Clear states
  • No dual authority paradox ‚Äî One sovereign at a time
  • No diluted stakes ‚Äî Coronation is make-or-break

Possible Outcomes

Scenario 1: Peaceful Succession

  1. King dies, Regent assumes power
  2. Heir is protected during coronation window
  3. Regent officiates coronation for Heir
  4. Heir crowned, becomes King
  5. Regent authority dissolves
  6. Stable transition

Scenario 2: Regent Usurpation

  1. King dies, Regent assumes power
  2. Regent delays coronation, builds support
  3. Heir becomes vulnerable
  4. Regent crowns themselves at coronation
  5. Heir's claim invalidated (or Heir eliminated)
  6. Contested reign begins

Scenario 3: Claimant Uprising

  1. King dies, Regent assumes power
  2. Claimant gathers supporters
  3. Challenges legitimacy of Heir
  4. Forces way into coronation or disrupts it
  5. Crowns themselves or backs different candidate
  6. Civil war potential

Scenario 4: Heir Assassination

  1. King dies, Regent assumes power
  2. Heir is assassinated during window
  3. Succession crisis deepens
  4. Regent must choose new heir or crown themselves
  5. Claimants emerge
  6. Maximum chaos

Interregnum: No Regent Exists

What if the King dies without appointing a Regent?

The realm enters Interregnum ‚Äî a true power vacuum.

During Interregnum:

  • No one has delegated King authority
  • Marshal or Magistrate may declare emergency Regent through court process
  • Coronation must happen immediately (no delay option)
  • Maximum chaos and opportunity for coups
  • Heir is most vulnerable

This is why appointing a Regent is crucial for succession planning.
