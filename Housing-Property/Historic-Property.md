## How Ownership Works

Historic properties are sold through **open public auction**.


- Anyone may bid
- All bids are visible
- The highest bidder wins control of the property
- The winner must establish guild presence by **placing a guildstone** within the purchased territory
- Upkeep begins immediately


> **â— Note**
> You buy control of an existing property.
> You bring your own guildstone and place it within the territory you now control.
> The guildstone marks your occupationâ€”it does not create the property.

**Guildstones mark occupation:**


- Player-created
- Player-placed
- Guild-owned

Once occupation is established, players may furnish, decorate, and operate the property as traditional home ownership allowsâ€”placing vendors, furniture, and other objects within the space they control.

**Historic sites themselves are:**


- Permanent world fixtures
- Independent of any guild's existence
- Continuously contestable

The guildstone is a marker of occupation, not construction.


## Claiming Is a Process, Not a Moment

Winning an auction does not instantly secure a historic property. It grants a **guild** the right to claim it.

After an auction concludes, the winning guild has a limited window to establish occupation by placing a guildstone within the territory. During this time, the location remains dangerous and contested â€” **deaths may delay the claim, but they do not invalidate it**.


> **Note**
> **Claims Belong to Guilds, Deaths Belong to Individuals**
>
> Historic property claims cannot be attached to a single mortal body.
> **Claims belong to guilds.
>             Deaths belong to individuals.**
> If a guild officer is killed while attempting to claim a property, the claim window continues. Another officer may complete the claim. The guild does not lose its right to the property simply because one member died.
> This ensures that historic sites are taken by **organization and persistence**, not by timing or grief.

If the claim is not completed within the window, the property returns to public auction.


### Property Sign Interaction, Not Guildstone Hauling

To establish occupation, a guild officer does not physically drag a guildstone across contested territory.

Instead:


- A property sign exists at or near the historic site
- A guild officer (with appropriate permissions) interacts with the sign
- The system validates that the guild won the auction and the claim window is active
- Upon confirmation, a guildstone is **automatically placed** within the property

This removes the logistical burden of guildstone transport while preserving the danger of the claim process. Officers must still reach the site, survive long enough to interact with the sign, and complete the claim under pressure.

**The risk remains. The tedium is removed.**


## One Guild, One Historic Property

A guild may only hold **one historic property at a time**.

This limitation is absolute. A guild cannot bid on, win, or claim a second historic property while already occupying one.


> **Note**
> **Why This Matters**
>
> This rule prevents empire-building through property hoarding.
> It forces meaningful choices:
> - Do you hold the Orc Fort for its defensive position?
> - Or abandon it to claim the Pirate Cove for its trade routes?
> - Do you keep the remote monastery?
> - Or compete for the prestigious keep?
> Guilds cannot accumulate historic sites like trophies. They must choose one and defend it.


### Automatic Relinquishment

If a guild currently occupying a historic property wins the auction for a different historic property, their existing property is **automatically relinquished** when they claim the new one.

The moment the guild officer interacts with the new property sign:


- The old guildstone is removed
- The previous property reverts to the world
- It is immediately listed for public auction at 1 gold starting bid

This ensures:


- No manual admin intervention required
- No exploitation through timing gaps
- Clean, immediate transitions
- Properties return to circulation instantly


> You cannot hold two forts.You cannot stockpile keeps.You choose one legacy at a time.


## Shell Guilds Don't Work

The Crown does not track guilds as abstract entities.

**The Crown tracks people.**

Recognition, standing, liens, seizure risk, and legal accountability are attached to **individuals**, not guild tags.


> **â— Note**
> If the same group of individuals operates multiple guilds:
> - The Crown recognizes the pattern
> - Recognition is withheld or revoked
> - Liens may be applied across all properties
> - Legal standing collapses

Britannia is not fooled by shell organizations.

The system recognizes:


- Guild leadership
- Active membership
- Overlapping rosters
- Coordinated bidding patterns

If you attempt to operate multiple guilds to claim multiple historic properties, you do not gain an empire. You gain vulnerability.

**Recognition tracks people, not banners.**

## Scale Matters

Not all historic properties are equal. Some locations are small, quiet, and easily held. Others are famous, valuable, and watched.

The larger and more significant a site is, the more pressure it attracts â€” economically, politically, and militarily. Holding such places requires coordination, presence, and endurance over time.

Britannia does not forbid solitary claimants. It simply tests whether they can survive what they have taken.


## City Property vs Historic Property

Property within Britain's city limits exists under law. Guards respond, violence has consequences, and stability is the norm. These locations are suited for individuals and small groups who wish to live, trade, and operate within protection.

Historic properties exist beyond that protection. They are visible, valuable, and contested. Holding them requires organization, defense, and endurance over time. Recognition is not assumed, and power is never secure.

Britannia does not rank these spaces. It distinguishes them. One offers safety through law. The other offers influence through risk.


## Crown Recognition: Political, Not Mechanical

Recognition is **political, not mechanical**.

Recognition affects:


- Trade access
- Legal standing
- Liens and seizure risk
- Narrative framing

But recognition does not determine who controls land.  

**Gold and survival do.**
