The Power of the Crown

The King is not "the strongest player" ‚Äî they are the one whose decisions cannot be ignored without consequence. The Crown embodies legitimacy and represents the idea that Britannia has a center.

Hard Powers

  • Appoint / remove high offices ‚Äî Regent, Marshal, Magistrate, and other key positions
  • Declare heirs ‚Äî Name legal successors to the throne
  • Grant / revoke land ‚Äî Control over specific royal properties through Land Commissioners and Stewards
  • Issue decrees ‚Äî Create binding commands and laws for the city. Enforcement through Guards.
  • Cancel executions and grant clemency
  • Preside over ceremonies ‚Äî Legitimize events through presence
  • Mediate disputes ‚Äî Final arbiter in player conflicts
  • Represent Britain to the world ‚Äî Diplomatic authority

Motivations

  • Stay in power
  • Be seen as rightful
  • Prevent civil collapse
  • Avoid being a puppet

Goals

  • Maintain loyalty of key offices
  • Keep order without appearing tyrannical
  • Control succession (or survive it)
  • Decide when to act ‚Äî and when not to

Natural Conflicts

The Crown is never truly secure. Power attracts ambition, and those closest to the throne are often the first to challenge it:

  • Regent ‚Äî Holds power during transitions, may refuse to relinquish it
  • Heirs ‚Äî Designated successors with official claims, may grow impatient or be manipulated
  • Claimants ‚Äî Contenders with merit, force, or political backing who seek to seize the throne
  • Marshal ‚Äî Controls the guards, may value order through force over political stability
  • Magistrate ‚Äî Interprets law and precedent, may use legal authority to challenge crown actions
  • High-ranking nobles ‚Äî Ambitious courtiers who undermine quietly, building support for their own plays

The question is never "if" the Crown will be challenged, but "when" and "by whom."

Player fantasy: Everyone watches what you do ‚Äî and what you don't.

The Core Principle

The crown is not inherited.
It is taken ‚Äî when the coronation succeeds.

In Kingdoms of Britannia, there is no automatic succession. When a king dies, the Crown seat becomes vacant. No one has mechanical "King" powers until a coronation ceremony successfully completes.

What happens between death and coronation is where politics, violence, and legitimacy collide.

Succession Overview: Coronation as Selection

üìú Upon the King's Death

The Crown is vacant. The Regent governs in trust. Named Heirs hold official claims in order of precedence. Claimants may assert contested claims through force or alliance.

The Crown transfers only at coronation, where any eligible contender‚ÄîHeir, Regent, or Claimant‚Äîmay be crowned.

Legitimacy is determined not by mechanics, but by who recognizes the outcome.

Coronation Is About Selection, Not Transfer

The coronation is not a ceremony that transfers power from the dead King to the Heir. It is a selection event where one person‚Äîwhoever successfully completes the ritual‚Äîbecomes the new King.

At the King's death, the following are eligible for coronation:

‚úÖ Named Heirs

Official, ordered eligibility

  • Primary Heir (first in line)
  • Secondary Heir (if Primary is dead or removed)
  • Tertiary Heir (if both above are unavailable)

Named Heirs have priority, not inevitability. Their claim is recognized, but coronation is not guaranteed.

‚öîÔ∏è Claimants

Unofficial, contested eligibility

Any player with a plausible claim to the throne‚Äîheritage, merit, conquest, or political backing‚Äîmay attempt coronation. Their claim is not officially recognized, but they can physically reach the coronation and attempt the ritual.

Claimants succeed through force, alliance, or by eliminating the Heir.

‚Üí See Claimants for complete details

‚öñÔ∏è The Regent

Procedural eligibility through custody of the state

The Regent holds all governing authority during the interregnum. They can officiate the coronation for the Heir‚Äîor crown themselves if circumstances allow. Their eligibility comes from already holding power, not from having a claim to the throne.

The Six Legitimate Paths to the Throne

A king can emerge through any of these paths. Most successful reigns rely on two or more.

üëë 1. Designated Heir (Default, Weakest Alone)

Who

Officially named heir by the reigning monarch.

Why It Works
  • Continuity and predictability
  • Court stability
  • Expected succession
Why It Fails
  • Heir is young, absent, unpopular, or weak
  • Heir lacks allies
  • Heir is implicated in scandal
  • Heir is accused of illegitimacy
Who Can Challenge an Heir
  • High offices (Marshal, Chancellor, Magistrate)
  • Bastards or blood claimants
  • Regents
  • Claimants "for stability"

Key Truth: A named heir has priority, not inevitability.

üèõÔ∏è 2. Regent / Protector of the Realm (Most Common in Practice)

Who

A high official temporarily empowered "until order is restored":

  • Marshal of the Guard
  • Chancellor
  • High Magistrate
  • Council-appointed figure
Why It Works
  • "Temporary" authority feels safe
  • Backed by institutions
  • Often controls guards or law
Why It Becomes Permanent
  • Crisis never "ends"
  • Regent proves competent
  • Heir falters or dies
  • People stop using "temporary"

This is how most coups actually happen. Regents don't seize power ‚Äî they inherit responsibility and never give it back.

üõ°Ô∏è 3. Force Claimant (Marshal / Guard-Backed)

Who
  • Marshal of the Guard
  • Popular Captain
  • Military-backed court figure
Why It Works
  • Guards enforce reality
  • Assassins fail
  • Dissent is contained
Why It's Dangerous
  • Lacks moral legitimacy
  • Fuels Minax narratives
  • Requires restraint to last

Force alone creates short reigns. This path succeeds only if paired with legal justification (Magistrate), narrative framing, or public support.

‚öñÔ∏è 4. Legal Claimant (Magistrate / Law Path)

Who
  • High Magistrate
  • Legal scholar backed by law
  • Council-backed interpreter of Virtue
How
  • Declares heir illegitimate
  • Voids succession due to broken oath
  • Interprets ancient precedent
Why It Works
  • Makes the coup "lawful"
  • Allows others to support without blood guilt
Why It's Fragile
  • Depends on belief in law
  • Vulnerable to force

Very Ultima-flavored: Virtue replaces divine right as the source of legitimacy.

ü©∏ 5. Blood Claimant (Bastard / Forgotten Line)

Who
  • Bastard of the Realm
  • Disgraced noble
  • Secret lineage reveal
Why It Works
  • Romantic appeal
  • Destabilizing
  • Explosive story potential
Why It Rarely Lasts
  • Requires proof
  • Invites rivals
  • Needs allies fast

Blood claims succeed only during chaos.

üåä 6. Popular Acclamation (Rare, Powerful)

Who
  • Charismatic commoner
  • Folk hero
  • Martyr figure
How
  • Crowd support
  • Guard hesitation
  • Court fractures
Why It Works

No one wants to oppose "the people." Violence against the beloved becomes illegitimate.

Why It's Rare
  • Requires momentum
  • Hard to sustain without institutions

This is how commoners become kings ‚Äî but only when the elite fails utterly.

The Regent: King-in-Function, Not King-in-Law

Core Rule: A Regent may rule. Only a crowned monarch may reign.

When the King dies, a Regent may automatically activate (if previously designated) or be assumed by a predefined role (Chancellor, Marshal, Council-appointed). The Regent gains Limited Crown Powers to keep Britain functioning.

What a Regent IS

  • The temporary wielder of crown authority
  • Responsible for stability
  • Able to issue orders, command guards, convene courts
  • Able to keep Britain running

What a Regent is NOT

  • Legitimate ruler
  • Guaranteed successor
  • Immune from challenge
  • Safe from coronation conflict
  • Protected from accusation

They hold power without legitimacy. That's the tension.

Limited King Powers

Regent CAN Regent CANNOT
Command the guard Declare themselves king by default
Maintain order Permanently alter succession
Issue temporary decrees Revoke noble status
Schedule a coronation Retroactively legitimize their reign
Appoint interim offices End the interregnum without coronation
Act "in the name of the realm" -

The Natural Conflict: Regent vs Heir

The Heir's Position:

  • "I am the rightful successor"
  • "The regent is overreaching"
  • "This delay is illegitimate"

The Regent's Position:

  • "I am keeping the realm safe"
  • "The heir is unfit / weak / absent"
  • "We cannot rush the crown"

Both are right. Neither is safe.

The Three Phases of Succession

A king is not decided in one moment. They pass through three distinct phases:

Phase 1: CLAIM (Social, No Mechanics)

This is pure RP. Someone becomes a claimant by:

  • Being named heir
  • Being appointed regent
  • Controlling the guard
  • Being publicly acclaimed
  • Declaring a claim backed by others

At this point: No mechanics trigger, no role is assigned, the throne is contested. This phase is intentionally messy.

Phase 2: CONSOLIDATION (Social Signals)

This is the most important phase. A claimant becomes inevitable when enough of the world behaves as if they are king.

Concrete signals that matter:

  • Guards obey their orders
  • Officials seek their approval
  • Decrees are followed
  • Trials are held in their name
  • Rivals stop pressing claims
  • Commoners accept or fear them

This phase can take hours, days, or a week. If there's still real dispute here, it is too early to engage mechanics.

Phase 3: RATIFICATION (Mechanical, Irreversible)

Only when Phase 2 is true do you flip the switch. This is the moment:

  • The King seat is assigned
  • Role-based permissions engage
  • Castle access, command authority, etc. turn on

This should feel like: "Oh. It finally happened."

Not: "The system decided."

The Coronation Ceremony

The Only Mechanical Gate: There is no king until a coronation succeeds.

Support should be RP'd. The outcome of the coronation ceremony determines who the king is. If multiple claimants arrive at the coronation ceremony expecting to be crowned king, there will be conflict.

The Rules (Simple and Brutal)

  • The ceremony happens at a specific location (Britain Castle throne room)
  • A Coronation NPC or object exists (Crown, throne, relic, banner)
  • Only one person can complete the coronation action
  • The action takes time (channeling / ritual / speech)
  • The action is interruptible
  • The action is public

That's it. No votes. No tallies. No UI polls.

What Happens If Multiple Claimants Show Up?

Case A: Only One Claimant Arrives

  • Ceremony completes
  • King role is assigned
  • Guards, court, and mechanics lock in

Clean, quiet, believable.

Case B: Multiple Claimants Arrive

Now it becomes Ultima Online at its best:

  • Arguments
  • Declarations
  • Guards choosing sides
  • Assassinations
  • Open battle
  • Chaos, but contained to a moment

Eventually:

  • One claimant is dead, flees, is arrested, or loses guard support
  • The surviving claimant completes the coronation
  • Mechanics engage
  • The crown becomes real

This is perfect RP.

Why Guards Decide the Outcome (And Why That's OK)

Guards don't auto-side with the heir. Guards side with whoever they've already been obeying. Which means:

  • All the RP before the coronation mattered
  • The ceremony just reveals the result

This mirrors history perfectly. Violence is political, not random.

What If the Coronation Fails?

Even better. If:

  • The heir dies
  • The hall turns into a massacre
  • Guards split
  • The ceremony can't complete

Then: Britain enters Interregnum. No king. Power fragments. Chaos escalates.

That's not a failure state. That's content.

Common Pretexts for Challenging an Heir

Why would someone say the heir is illegitimate? Here are believable reasons:

  • The heir was born before a marriage
  • The heir was secretly adopted
  • The heir broke an oath
  • The heir committed a crime
  • The heir is unfit (madness, cowardice, heresy)
  • The naming decree was invalid
  • The king was coerced
Important: The truth does not matter. Only whether people accept the claim.

Who Can Step Into the Throne?

Anyone who can convince three of these five to support them:

  1. üõ° The Guard
  2. ‚öñÔ∏è The Law
  3. üñã The Narrative (Court opinion)
  4. ü§ù Key Offices (Chancellor, Advisors)
  5. üåä The Public (or at least prevent unrest)

The heir usually starts with 1‚Äì2. A successful challenger assembles 3+.

Summary: The Single Rule

The throne does not pass automatically.
It passes to whoever can make their rule unquestioned.
Secondary Rule: A king is not chosen by the system. The system only acknowledges a king once the world already has.

What this means in practice:

  • Kings can reign for months
  • Heirs are never "safe"
  • Commoners can ascend
  • Force isn't enough
  • Law matters but can be bent
  • Narrative is a weapon

Most importantly: Succession is not a mechanic. It is a crisis that players must navigate.

Why This Won't Anger the Player Base

Players aren't mad when they lost. They're mad when they feel cheated.

This system avoids that because:

  • The struggle was visible
  • The signals were clear
  • The ratification matched reality

People might say: "I hated that king."

But they won't say: "The system screwed us."

That's the difference.
