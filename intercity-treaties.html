The Diplomatic Fabric of the Realm

Treaties are not guarantees. They are statements of intent.
The question is not whether they hold. It is what breaks them.

Core Principle

Treaties are formal agreements between city-states that define their relationship, obligations, and expectations. They are political instruments, not mechanical laws.

Treaties are NOT:

  • ‚ùå Magically enforced contracts
  • ‚ùå Immunity from betrayal
  • ‚ùå Permanent or irrevocable bonds
  • ‚ùå Auto-triggered military alliances

Treaties ARE:

  • ‚úÖ Publicly visible commitments
  • ‚úÖ Tools for coordination and alignment
  • ‚úÖ Frameworks for expectations
  • ‚úÖ Political ammunition when broken
  • ‚úÖ Narrative structure for realm politics

Treaty vs. Fealty

Fealty is a specific type of treaty, but treaties encompass much more:

Type Structure Example
Fealty Asymmetric (liege/vassal) Yew swears fealty to Britain, pays tribute
Alliance Symmetric (mutual defense) Yew & Minoc pledge mutual military support
Neutrality Pact Symmetric (non-aggression) Britain & Vesper agree not to attack each other
Trade Agreement Symmetric (economic cooperation) Trinsic & Moonglow reduce tariffs on merchants
Recognition Treaty Asymmetric (legitimacy endorsement) Britain recognizes Vesper's new ruler

See: Fealty & Sovereignty for the fealty-specific mechanics

Types of Treaties

1. Military Alliance

Mutual Defense Pact

Commitment: Both cities agree to defend each other if attacked.

Terms May Include:

  • Response time expectations (e.g., "within 24 hours")
  • Definition of "attack" (siege, raid, assassination?)
  • Scope of support (full military, guards only, supplies only?)
  • Exclusions (e.g., "not applicable if you attack first")

Not Mechanically Enforced:

  • No auto-deployment of guards
  • No forced combat participation
  • Ally can choose not to help

But Politically Costly if Broken:

  • Public breach declaration
  • Loss of trust from other potential allies
  • Narrative weapon for the abandoned ally
  • Possible retaliation or sanctions

2. Neutrality Pact (Non-Aggression)

Mutual Non-Aggression Treaty

Commitment: Both cities agree not to attack, raid, or support enemies of each other.

Terms May Include:

  • Definition of "aggression" (guards, bounties, harboring fugitives?)
  • Duration (1 year, indefinite, until revoked?)
  • Exceptions (e.g., "unless you attack our allies")
  • Buffer zones or neutral territory definitions

Use Cases:

  • Two cities want to avoid conflict but not be allies
  • Temporary ceasefire during internal crises
  • Detente between rival powers

Breaking neutrality is less severe than breaking alliance, but still politically visible.

3. Trade Agreement

Economic Cooperation Treaty

Commitment: Both cities agree to economic terms that benefit merchants or treasuries.

Possible Terms:

  • Reduced tariffs or taxes on cross-city trade
  • Mutual recognition of vendor licenses
  • Shared market access or trade routes
  • Price controls or monopolies
  • Resource exchange agreements (food, ore, wood)

Enforcement:

  • Primarily player-driven (merchants care about this)
  • Violation = economic retaliation (tariffs, blockades)
  • Can create internal factional conflict (merchants vs. isolationists)

4. Recognition Treaty

Political Legitimacy Endorsement

Commitment: One city formally recognizes another's ruler, government, or territorial claim.

Use Cases:

  • New king needs international legitimacy
  • Contested succession resolved by external backing
  • City claims sovereignty over disputed territory

Asymmetric but Valuable:

  • Recognized party gains prestige and stability
  • Recognizing party gains influence over recognized party
  • Can be revoked if ruler changes

Related but distinct from Capital Recognition (ideological) ‚Äî this is bilateral and specific.

5. Extradition Treaty

Cross-Border Justice Agreement

Commitment: Cities agree to honor each other's arrest warrants and extradite fugitives.

Terms May Include:

  • Which crimes qualify (murder yes, unpaid taxes no?)
  • Evidence requirements before extradition
  • Asylum exceptions (political crimes, religious persecution)
  • Mutual guard cooperation in cross-border pursuits

Political Minefield:

  • Harboring fugitives can strain relations
  • Refusing extradition can be justified or denounced
  • Creates refugee crises when cities refuse cooperation

6. Tribute Treaty (Without Fealty)

Economic Submission Without Political Fealty

Commitment: One city pays another for protection or access, but without formal fealty relationship.

Use Cases:

  • Weak city pays stronger city for non-aggression
  • City pays toll for trade route access
  • Temporary payment to avoid invasion

Distinct from Fealty:

  • No symbolic submission
  • No political endorsement
  • Purely transactional

Treaty Negotiation Process

Step 1: Proposal

One city initiates treaty negotiations:

  • Emissary delivers terms to target city's ruler
  • Proposal includes: Treaty type, terms, duration, consequences
  • Public or private: Some negotiations are secret until signed

Step 2: Negotiation

Cities may counter-propose or adjust terms:

  • Direct negotiation: Rulers meet in person or via messenger
  • Public debate: Some treaties are negotiated openly (risky)
  • Mediation: Third-party city or neutral figure facilitates

Negotiation Window

Proposals expire after 7 days if no response.

Either party may walk away during negotiation with no penalty.

Once signed, breaking the treaty is politically costly.

Step 3: Signing

Both parties formally agree to the treaty:

  • Logged in Treaty Ledger (see Fealty Ledger)
  • Public announcement fires across the realm
  • Terms become visible to all players
  • Treaty takes effect immediately

Step 4: Enforcement (Political, Not Mechanical)

Treaties are not auto-enforced. Compliance is a choice.

Critical: The system does not prevent treaty violations.
It only makes them visible and politically consequential.

Treaty Duration & Renewal

Treaties can be structured with different lifespans:

Duration Type Description Use Case
Indefinite Lasts until revoked by either party Long-term alliances, fealty relationships
Fixed Term Expires after X months/years Temporary non-aggression, trade trials
Conditional Ends when condition met (e.g., "until war ends") Wartime alliances, crisis cooperation
Auto-Renewing Renews unless explicitly revoked Standing agreements that should persist

Renewal Negotiation

When a fixed-term treaty nears expiration:

  • 30 days before expiration: Both parties notified
  • Renegotiation window opens: Terms can be adjusted
  • If no agreement: Treaty expires, cities revert to neutral status
  • If renewed: New terms logged, announcement fires

Multi-Party Treaties

Treaties can involve more than two cities.

Example: Three-Way Alliance

The Northern Pact

Signatories: Yew, Minoc, Vesper

Terms:

  • Mutual defense against Britain
  • Shared trade routes & tariff reductions
  • No separate peace with Britain without unanimous consent

Political Dynamics:

  • Stronger together than alone
  • Risk of one city defecting for better terms
  • Coordination challenges (who leads?)

Coalition Fragility

Multi-party treaties are inherently unstable:

  • Defection incentives: One city breaks off for individual gain
  • Free-rider problem: Some cities benefit without contributing
  • Coordination costs: Harder to agree on action
  • Blame dynamics: Who violated first?

This creates emergent betrayal gameplay.

Treaty Breach & Consequences

What Counts as Breach?

Depends on treaty terms, but common violations include:

  • Military: Attacking treaty partner, aiding their enemies
  • Economic: Violating trade terms, imposing sanctions
  • Political: Backing rival claimants, harboring fugitives
  • Territorial: Violating borders, occupying treaty zones

Declaration of Breach

When one party believes the other has violated the treaty:

  1. Public accusation: "Yew has violated our treaty by [specific act]"
  2. Evidence presented: Logs, witnesses, documentation
  3. Response demanded: Explanation, compensation, or acknowledgment
  4. If unresolved: Treaty declared void, relations hostile
Breach declarations are political weapons.
Even false accusations damage reputations. Use carefully.

Consequences of Breach

No automatic penalties, but political fallout:

  • Reputation damage: Future treaty partners hesitate
  • Retaliation: Injured party may respond militarily or economically
  • Coalition pressure: Allies of injured party may shun violator
  • Narrative ammunition: "They cannot be trusted"

Treaty Termination

Formal Withdrawal

Either party may terminate a treaty formally:

  • Notice period: Typically 30 days advance warning
  • Public announcement: Realm-wide notification
  • Reasons stated (or not): Political choice
  • Treaty ends cleanly: No automatic hostility

Formal withdrawal is less damaging than breach, but still politically visible.

Hostile Termination

One party ends the treaty abruptly with hostile intent:

  • No notice period
  • Often coupled with attack or sanctions
  • Treated as breach by other party
  • Creates instant hostility

Automatic Termination

Some events automatically void treaties:

  • City deactivates: Falls below population threshold
  • Ruler death (if specified): Treaty tied to individual
  • Conditional trigger met: (e.g., "until war ends" ‚Äî war ends)
  • Both parties agree: Mutual dissolution

Treaties & Power Blocs

Over time, treaties create emergent power structures:

Scenario: Realm Fractures

The Capital Coalition

Britain (liege) + Yew + Trinsic (vassals)

  • Fealty agreements with tribute
  • Mutual defense pact
  • Extradition treaties

The Free Cities Alliance

Vesper + Minoc + Moonglow (equals)

  • Mutual defense pact
  • Shared trade agreements
  • Neutrality pact with Skara Brae

Neutral / Unaligned

Skara Brae (isolated by choice)

  • No treaties with anyone
  • Vulnerable but independent

Result: Realm-level tensions between blocs. Proxy conflicts. Diplomatic crises.

Common Questions

Q: Can I sign contradictory treaties?

A: Mechanically yes, but politically disastrous. Example: Alliance with Yew AND alliance with Yew's enemy Minoc. When war breaks out, you must choose ‚Äî and one treaty breaks.

Q: Do treaties prevent me from attacking?

A: No. You can still attack a treaty partner. But it will be logged as breach, publicly visible, and politically costly.

Q: Can I negotiate in secret?

A: Negotiations can be private, but once signed, treaties are always public. Secret treaties violate the visibility principle.

Q: What if my ally refuses to help when I'm attacked?

A: You can declare breach and terminate the alliance. Other cities will see that your ally abandoned you. This damages their reputation.

Q: Can treaties involve NPCs or admin-controlled cities?

A: Only if admin chooses to roleplay an NPC city government. Treaties are designed for player-ruled cities.

Q: Do treaties expire when a ruler dies?

A: Depends on treaty terms. Can be tied to individual ("with King X") or to city ("with Britain, regardless of ruler").

Design Intent

Treaties create:

  • Coordinated diplomacy without script enforcement
  • Public commitments that pressure behavior
  • Emergent alliances that shift over time
  • Betrayal incentives when terms become inconvenient
  • Political history that accumulates reputation
You get international relations gameplay without a treaty enforcement engine.
