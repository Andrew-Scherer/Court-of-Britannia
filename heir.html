"Claim Without Authority ‚Äî Blood Without Power"

Core Principle: The Heir Presumptive holds legitimacy, claim, and blood right ‚Äî but NO King powers. They are the next in line, not the ruler. The Crown transfers only at coronation.

üìú What Is an Heir?

An Heir is a player appointed by the King to be a designated successor to the throne. A King may name up to three heirs, ordered by precedence:

  • Primary Heir: The favored successor ‚Äî strongest legitimacy and first right to coronation
  • Secondary Heir: The legitimate backup ‚Äî inherits claim if Primary dies or is disqualified
  • Tertiary Heir: The last resort ‚Äî ensures continuity even in catastrophic succession crises

These appointments signal:

  • Legitimacy: The King's official endorsement of these persons as next rulers
  • Ordered Precedence: Clear hierarchy of who has the strongest claim
  • Dynasty Survival: Multiple heirs protect against permadeath eliminating the line of succession
  • Future Authority: The promise of power ‚Äî after coronation

However, being named Heir does not grant any King powers. While the King lives, no Heir has authority to rule Britannia. They are claimants, not rulers.

üëë Why Name Multiple Heirs?

In a world of permadeath, succession is never guaranteed. A King names multiple heirs for strategic reasons:

1. Insurance Against Permadeath

If the Primary Heir dies permanently (assassination, battle, accident), the dynasty doesn't collapse. The Secondary Heir immediately becomes the strongest claimant.

2. Continuity During Crisis

Multiple heirs ensure that even if two successors die, a third remains to stabilize the realm and prevent total power vacuum.

3. Political Balance

Naming heirs from different factions can balance competing powers and prevent any single faction from dominating succession.

4. Hedge Against Betrayal

If the Primary Heir proves disloyal, incompetent, or compromised, the King has alternatives without abandoning succession plans entirely.

5. Fear of Extinction

Kings who fear their dynasty will die with them often name multiple heirs to ensure someone carries their legacy forward.

‚ö†Ô∏è The Cost of Multiple Heirs: While multiple heirs increase dynasty survival, they reduce succession certainty. More heirs means more potential conflict, contested claims, and political maneuvering during succession. Each additional heir is both protection and danger.

‚öîÔ∏è When the King Dies

When a King dies, NO heir automatically becomes King. Succession is not automatic. Instead:

  1. The Crown becomes vacant ‚Äî no one holds the Crown
  2. All named heirs become eligible candidates ‚Äî Primary, Secondary, and Tertiary heirs are all recognized claimants
  3. Heir precedence determines claim strength ‚Äî Primary has strongest claim, then Secondary, then Tertiary
  4. If a Regent exists, they govern in trust ‚Äî holding delegated authority until coronation
  5. A coronation timer begins (3-7 days) ‚Äî creating urgency to get crowned
  6. Coronation resolves which heir (if any) is crowned ‚Äî the first to coronation wins the Crown
‚ö†Ô∏è Critical: All heirs hold claim and legitimacy, but zero King powers. They cannot issue edicts, command guards, or enforce laws until crowned. They are symbols without authority. The Crown transfers only at coronation.

Succession Scenarios: What Happens to Each Heir?

Scenario 1: All Three Heirs Survive

  • All three are eligible candidates with ordered precedence
  • Primary Heir has strongest claim and political advantage
  • Secondary and Tertiary may challenge, ally with Primary, or attempt their own coronation
  • Maximum succession tension ‚Äî three legitimate claimants competing for the Crown

Scenario 2: Primary Heir Dies

  • Secondary Heir inherits strongest claim and becomes presumptive successor
  • Tertiary Heir becomes the new backup heir
  • Political landscape shifts ‚Äî Secondary now has Primary's legitimacy

Scenario 3: Primary and Secondary Both Die

  • Tertiary Heir becomes the sole named heir with strongest claim
  • Dynasty survives catastrophic losses
  • Tertiary's legitimacy is now undisputed among named heirs

Scenario 4: All Three Heirs Die (Extinction Event)

  • No named heirs remain ‚Äî total succession collapse
  • Claimants (unofficial rivals) become the only path to succession
  • Regent (if any) may seize the throne or civil war may erupt
  • This is the nightmare scenario Kings name multiple heirs to prevent
‚ö†Ô∏è Permadeath Risk: In a permadeath environment, multiple heirs are not paranoia ‚Äî they are dynasty insurance. Assassination, battle, and accident can eliminate any claimant. Three heirs mean three chances to preserve the line of succession.

üõ°Ô∏è What the Heir Presumptive Has

‚úÖ The Heir Holds:

  • Strongest Claim: Official designation as next ruler
  • Legitimacy: Recognized by the previous King and (usually) the realm
  • Blood Right: Lineage-based or merit-based claim endorsed by the Crown
  • Symbolic Authority: Respect, deference, and political weight ‚Äî but no enforcement power
  • First Right to Coronation: Priority access to the coronation process

‚ùå The Heir Does NOT Hold:

  • The Crown: Only transfers at coronation
  • King Powers: Cannot issue laws, commands, or enforce authority
  • Guaranteed Succession: Must survive rivals, Regent, and Claimants to reach coronation
  • Regent Powers: If a Regent exists, they hold delegated King authority ‚Äî not the Heir

üéØ The Heir's Challenges

An Heir Presumptive faces multiple threats on the path to coronation:

1. The Regent (Power Without Claim)

If a Regent exists, they may:

  • Block coronation: Use their authority to delay or prevent the Heir from being crowned
  • Seize the throne: Self-crown before the Heir can act
  • Manipulate the Heir: Demand concessions, alliances, or loyalty in exchange for allowing coronation
  • Protect the Heir: (Rare) Genuinely serve as a steward until the Heir is crowned

2. Claimants (Contested Blood)

Claimants are rival players with contested or unofficial claims to the throne. They may:

  • Challenge the Heir's legitimacy
  • Raise armies to contest succession
  • Race to reach coronation first
  • Attempt to assassinate the Heir

See Claimants for more on how contested claims work.

3. The Coronation Timer (3-7 Days)

The longer the Heir waits, the more unstable the realm becomes:

  • Rivals gain ground: Claimants and Regent may seize momentum
  • Chaos spreads: Without a crowned King, the realm fractures
  • Accidents happen: Political assassination, ambush, imprisonment
‚è≥ Time Pressure: The coronation timer forces the Heir to act quickly ‚Äî alliance-building, securing power, or racing to the coronation before rivals strike.

4. Assassination & Imprisonment

As the recognized successor, the Heir is a high-value target:

  • Rivals may kill the Heir to clear their path to the throne
  • A Regent may imprison the Heir to consolidate power
  • Claimants may ambush the Heir before they reach coronation

üèÜ Paths to the Throne

The Heir Presumptive has several paths to claim the Crown:

1. Peaceful Succession (Best Case)

  • The Regent (if any) steps aside or supports the Heir
  • No serious Claimants contest the succession
  • The Heir reaches coronation safely and becomes King/Queen
‚úÖ Ideal Outcome: Smooth transfer of power. The Heir's legitimacy is undisputed, and they are crowned without conflict.

2. Political Negotiation

  • The Heir must negotiate with the Regent for support or non-interference
  • May involve promises, alliances, or concessions (e.g., "I'll keep you as Regent if you let me crown")
  • The Heir leverages their legitimacy to build coalitions

3. Race to Coronation

  • The Heir rushes to complete the coronation before the Regent or Claimants act
  • High-risk, high-reward ‚Äî success means becoming King, failure means losing power
  • May involve bodyguards, secret routes, or surprise timing

4. Conflict & Uprising

  • If the Regent blocks coronation, the Heir may rally support for an uprising
  • Claimants may force the Heir into open conflict
  • The Heir's legitimacy becomes a rallying cry for loyalists to overthrow the Regent

See Becoming King for full coronation mechanics.

ü§ù Why a King Appoints Heirs

A wise King appoints heirs for strategic reasons:

1. Ensure Smooth Succession

Without named heirs, the King's death creates chaos. Unofficial Claimants may emerge, leading to civil war. Official heirs reduce uncertainty and provide legitimate succession paths.

2. Legitimize the Next Ruler

The King's endorsement grants heirs symbolic authority and makes them the presumptive next rulers in the eyes of the realm. This legitimacy is crucial during succession struggles.

3. Control Succession

Appointing heirs ensures the Crown passes to someone the King trusts ‚Äî whether by blood, merit, or loyalty. Multiple heirs allow the King to balance different factions.

4. Prevent Power Vacuums

Heirs act as backup rulers if the King dies unexpectedly, reducing the risk of long-term Interregnum. Multiple heirs mean multiple safeguards against total succession collapse.

5. Political Signaling

Appointing heirs sends a message to the realm about who the King favors, creating alliances and deterring rivals. The order of precedence signals the King's priorities and strategic alignments.

6. Dynasty Insurance (Permadeath)

In a permadeath environment, no successor is guaranteed to survive. Multiple heirs are survival insurance ‚Äî if one dies, another carries the dynasty forward. This is not paranoia, it's pragmatism.

‚ùì Can Heirs Be Revoked or Reordered?

Yes. While the King lives, they have absolute control over heir appointments:

What the King Can Do:

  • Revoke any Heir status ‚Äî remove Primary, Secondary, or Tertiary at will
  • Reorder heir precedence ‚Äî promote Secondary to Primary, demote Primary to Tertiary, etc.
  • Replace heirs entirely ‚Äî appoint new heirs based on shifting politics or loyalty
  • Reduce the number of heirs ‚Äî go from three heirs to one, or eliminate all heirs entirely
  • Punish disloyalty ‚Äî remove Heir designation as political punishment
Strategic Heir Management: A King may deliberately reorder heirs as a political signal ‚Äî promoting loyal heirs, demoting suspected traitors, or balancing competing factions by shifting precedence.

After the King Dies:

Once the King dies, all heir designations are locked in. The precedence at the moment of death determines claim strength during succession. No one can revoke or reorder heirs after the King is dead ‚Äî only coronation resolves who becomes the next King.
