⚔️ Guild Interactions

Simple Mechanics, No Adjudication — Guilds Define Their Own Relationships

The Core Principle

Guilds are free to define their relationships with each other using simple mechanics; no system exists to resolve or sanitize the consequences of those choices.

What Guilds Can Do

Guilds interact using direct, bilateral mechanics:

Available Guild Mechanics

  • Declare war — Bilateral hostility state
  • Declare alliance — Bilateral cooperation signal
  • Maintain independent relationships — Each guild pair has its own status
  • Change relationships instantly — No cooldowns, no permissions required

Critically:

  • Guild A can ally with Guild B
  • Guild A can war with Guild C
  • Guild A can do both simultaneously
  • Guilds can war despite sharing a faction banner
  • Guilds can ally without sharing a faction banner

Faction membership does not override guild relationships.

Alignment ≠ behavior. Banner ≠ trust. War ≠ total opposition.

What Guilds Cannot Do

You explicitly do NOT get:

  • ❌ Binding treaties
  • ❌ Enforced arbitration
  • ❌ Guild courts
  • ❌ Formalized diplomacy layers
  • ❌ Multi-party recognition systems
  • ❌ Fealty trees

Why these are excluded: They would create a parallel civic system, undermining Britain as the sole source of law.

The Key Distinction

This Is NOT "No Mechanics"

It's: "No higher-order civic system."

Allowed: Direct, bilateral declarations between guilds

Disallowed: Meta-systems that sit above guilds and adjudicate outcomes

Guilds are pre-civic. They existed before law. War and alliance are ancient.

What's missing is not the ability to interact — it's the ability to appeal to authority above Britain.

How Guilds Interact (In Practice)

Important: Factions do not act. Guilds act. Factions are context, not actors.

1️⃣ Territorial Conflict (The Primary Mode)

Guilds interact first and foremost through space.

  • Raids
  • Sieges
  • Ambushes
  • Occupation attempts
  • Harassment of vendors or supply lines

The mechanics involved are:

  • Upkeep
  • Death
  • Loss of control

If a guild can't defend or afford its land, it loses it.

That's the interaction.

2️⃣ Economic Pressure

Guild A can hurt Guild B by:

  • Undercutting vendors
  • Blocking trade routes
  • Forcing higher security costs
  • Targeting crafters or suppliers
  • Driving up upkeep indirectly through conflict

There is no "economic warfare button."
There is only making existence more expensive.

Gold is the battlefield.

3️⃣ Alliances (Bilateral and Situational)

Guilds can:

  • Coordinate attacks
  • Share intelligence
  • Temporarily cooperate
  • Betray each other instantly

Alliances exist outside the faction system:

  • Guild alliances are bilateral (A ↔ B)
  • Faction membership is collective ("We fly this banner")
  • Neither overrides the other

This keeps alliances fragile and real.

Critical insight: "The enemy of my enemy is my friend" can exist within or across faction lines.

4️⃣ Britain as the Interpreter

This is the critical layer.

Guild conflict matters when Britain reacts.

Britain is:

  • An amplifier
  • A reframer
  • A memory

Britain is NOT:

  • A permission gate
  • A mediator
  • A referee

Examples:

  • Attacking a recognized guild risks outlawry
  • Harboring an outlawed guild risks sanctions
  • Repeated aggression may cause Britain to:
    • Withdraw recognition
    • Refuse trade
    • Justify bounties
    • Endorse retaliation

Britain doesn't mediate.
Britain interprets.

Guild A doesn't need permission to attack Guild B.
But Britain decides what that attack means politically.

5️⃣ Legitimacy Warfare (Soft Power)

Guilds fight over:

  • Being recognized by Britain
  • Staying recognized
  • Getting rivals outlawed

So guild interaction often looks like:

"We don't need to destroy them — we just need Britain to stop recognizing them."

This turns the following into guild weapons:

  • Assassination
  • False-flag attacks
  • Evidence planting
  • Narrative control

Again: no adjudication system required.
Only visibility and interpretation.

6️⃣ Outlaw vs Outlaw Dynamics

Outlawed guilds are the wild west.

  • No legal protection from Britain
  • No appeal process
  • Violence is expected
  • Reputation matters more than law

This creates:

  • Pirate politics
  • Criminal syndicate rivalries
  • Monster guilds fighting over territory

Britain doesn't care unless it spills inward.

The Relationship Between Guilds and Factions

Remember the Hierarchy

Guilds act.
Britain interprets.
Factions contextualize.

Factions are one alignment signal, not the only one.

A faction banner tells you:

  • "These guilds claim shared identity"

It does NOT tell you:

  • Whether they actually cooperate
  • Whether they're at war internally
  • Whether they have external alliances

Why This Design Works

  • ✅ Alliances are real because they're fragile
  • ✅ Betrayal is easy and meaningful
  • ✅ Power comes from coordination, not UI
  • ✅ Britain stays central without micromanaging
  • ✅ Players create politics instead of navigating menus
  • ✅ "The enemy of my enemy is my friend" emerges naturally
  • ✅ Faction banners can mask internal wars or external alliances

This is how UO should feel.

The Clean Rule

Lock This In

Guilds define relationships directly.
No system exists to resolve or sanitize the consequences.
Britain interprets what those relationships mean politically.

Or even simpler:

Guilds fight.
Britain remembers.

Final Sanity Check

Your World Now Has:

  • One source of law: Britain
  • Many sources of power: Guilds (territorial, economic, military)
  • One universal currency of survival: Gold
  • One universal currency of legitimacy: Recognition
  • Simple relationship mechanics: War and alliance
  • Zero adjudication layers: No treaties, courts, or binding arbitration

Summary

Guilds interact with each other using simple, direct mechanics: war declarations, alliances, territorial conflict, economic pressure, and violence. These relationships exist independently of faction membership—guilds can ally across faction lines or war within them.

What does NOT exist: treaties, courts, arbitration, or formalized diplomatic systems. No mechanism exists to resolve or sanitize the consequences of guild choices.

Britain interprets guild actions politically—recognition, toleration, or outlawry—which reframes what those actions mean. Britain is an amplifier and memory, not a mediator or referee.

Guilds are free to define their relationships. No system exists to resolve the consequences. Britain decides what those relationships mean.

Guilds fight. Britain remembers.
