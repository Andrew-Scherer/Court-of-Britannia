Land as Scarce Political Resource

Core Philosophy

Land is not a reward for logging in. It is a political asset with weight, risk, and consequence.

The Fundamental Rule:

More property = more exposure. No one accumulates land without accumulating risk.

Why Stewards Cannot Assign at Whim

If Stewards could freely assign properties:

  • üî¥ Favoritism accusations explode
  • üî¥ Politics collapses into "who knows the Steward"
  • üî¥ Land stops feeling earned or risky
  • üî¥ The system loses legitimacy instantly
Design Victory: Stewards never choose winners. They only execute predefined allocation methods.

This keeps Stewards:

  • ‚úÖ Socially important (everyone needs them)
  • ‚úÖ Politically pressured (everyone wants influence)
  • ‚úÖ Mechanically constrained (cannot play favorites)

Property Assignment Methods

Every property has exactly one assignment method. This is metadata on the building. Stewards execute the method; they do not choose it.

Method 1: Royal Grant

Used for:

  • Noble house manors
  • Strategic buildings
  • Political rewards
  • Punishments (revocation)

Who decides: King or Regent

Steward does:

  1. Records the grant in Land Ledger
  2. Assigns property role to designated recipient
  3. Announces publicly

Why this exists: This is how land becomes leverage. Kings need rewards besides gold and offices beyond Marshal.

üí° Political Impact

Royal grants create:

  • Patronage networks
  • Visible favoritism (intentional pressure)
  • Revocation leverage (take it back to humiliate)
  • House anchors (land = legitimacy)

Method 2: Auction (Public Competition) ‚úÖ

Used for:

  • Small cottages
  • Shops
  • Farms
  • "Upward mobility" properties

Who decides: Rules decide (highest bid)

Steward does:

  1. Announces auction publicly
  2. Collects bids (or system collects)
  3. Declares winner per rules (no discretion)
  4. Assigns property role

Why this exists: Gives commoners a path to land that isn't royal favor. Creates non-violent competition. Drains accumulated wealth/influence.

Important: The Steward does NOT pick the winner, negotiate bids, or interpret offers. They are a clerk, not a patron.

Method 3: Internal Allocation

Used for:

  • Noble house properties
  • Guild halls
  • Political faction residences
  • Office buildings (guard barracks, court, etc.)

Who decides: The unit itself

Steward does:

  1. Assigns the property role to the unit
  2. Does not care who the Primary is
  3. Records the allocation

Why this exists: Units must manage themselves. The city does not meddle in house succession or guild leadership unless eviction/exile occurs.

No interference. No favoritism. Internal politics stay internal until they become crimes or scandals.

Method 4: Automatic Reversion

Used for:

  • Abandoned properties (X days vacant)
  • Dead individual role-holders (with no heir/successor)
  • Failed auctions

Who decides: Time and system rules

Steward does:

  1. Nothing until the property reverts to OPEN
  2. Then announces availability for auction or grant

Why this exists: Keeps the map clean. Prevents dead storage. Forces land back into circulation.

Multiple Property Ownership

The critical design question: Should players and units be allowed to hold more than one property?

Answer: Yes ‚Äî but with hard constraints.

‚ö†Ô∏è Individual Player Limits (Recommended)

Property Type Max Holdings
Primary Residence (cottage, townhouse) 1
Secondary Property (shop, farm, workspace) 0‚Äì1
Total Maximum 2

Why these limits:

  • Prevents hoarding
  • Preserves upward mobility for others
  • Keeps cottages circulating
  • Still allows personal ambition

Enforcement: Simple. Auction UI refuses bids if player already holds max. Steward assignment checks the same rule. No judgment calls required.

üè∞ Unit Limits (Noble Houses, Guilds, Political Factions)

Units are political entities; they should sprawl ‚Äî but visibly.

Unit Type Primary Auxiliary
Noble House 1 Manor 0‚Äì2 (shop, warehouse, safehouse)
Guild 1 Hall 0‚Äì1 (worksite, storage)
House (political faction) 1 Residence 0‚Äì2 (meeting hall, safehouse)
Office As defined by role Barracks, court, archive, jail

Why asymmetric limits:

  • Power becomes visible (everyone knows who holds what)
  • Loss becomes painful (evict a house = lose multiple sites)
  • Eviction becomes meaningful (strips influence, not just one door)
  • Ambition has room (but not infinite sprawl)
Critical Rule: Each additional property increases exposure to eviction and loss. More land = more targets. This is intentional.

How Auctions Work (Keep Them Simple)

You do not want a deep economy sim. Auctions must be straightforward or they'll consume admin time and create endless disputes.

Simple Auction Model (Recommended)

  1. Auction Duration: Fixed window (e.g., 48 hours real time)
  2. Bids Accepted:
    • Gold (simple)
    • OR Prestige tokens (abstract currency)
    • OR Favor markers (political capital)
  3. Winner: Highest bid wins
  4. Tiebreaker: Earliest bid wins
  5. No Negotiation: No extensions. No counter-offers. No drama inside the system.

All drama happens outside the auction.

  • Players form coalitions to pool bids
  • Noble houses pressure rivals to withdraw
  • Kings quietly fund favorites
  • Minax sabotages bidders

Why Public Bids Matter

All bids are announced publicly (or at minimum, visible to other bidders). This creates:

  • Pressure to outbid
  • Social consequence (everyone knows who wants what)
  • Legitimacy (no secret deals)
  • RP fuel (rivalries emerge naturally)

Eviction + Auctions = Political Engine

This is where property ownership, auctions, and eviction combine into a coherent political system.

Scenario: House Eviction ‚Üí Auction

  1. House Hawkwright is evicted for harboring an exile
  2. Their manor returns to the Crown
  3. King decides:
    • Option A: Re-grant it politically to House Rowan (rewards loyalty)
    • Option B: Auction it publicly (humiliates Hawkwright, enriches treasury, creates opportunity for commoners)
  4. If auctioned:
    • Hawkwright cannot bid (exiled/disgraced)
    • Rivals scramble to claim their ancestral seat
    • Commoner guilds pool resources
    • Auction becomes public theater
  5. Winner takes the manor ‚Äî and all the symbolic weight

This is devastating without killing anyone.

It creates:

  • Non-violent punishment (eviction)
  • Social mobility (auction)
  • Wealth drain (bids remove currency)
  • Narrative consequence (who lives where matters)

Integration with Other Systems

Permadeath Integration

  • Individual dies ‚Üí property SEALED ‚Üí grace period (72h) ‚Üí reverts to OPEN if no successor
  • If property method = Auction ‚Üí goes back to auction pool
  • If property method = Royal Grant ‚Üí King decides fate

Noble House Integration

  • Noble house properties use Internal Allocation method
  • Primary holder changes internally; Steward only cares that the house still exists
  • If a noble house dissolves ‚Üí property reverts to Crown ‚Üí King chooses Royal Grant or Auction

Exile Integration

  • Exiled individuals cannot bid in auctions
  • Exiled individuals cannot receive Royal Grants (unless King explicitly pardons)
  • Units harboring exiles risk eviction (which strips all properties)

Role System Integration

  • Property ownership is always role-bound
  • Individuals enter roles ("Farmer #3", "Shopkeeper - South Market")
  • Units hold collective roles ("Rowan Manor", "Guard Barracks")
  • Access auto-updates when roles change

Common Questions

Q: Can a commoner outbid a noble house?

A: Yes. Auctions are merit-based (highest bid wins). If a wealthy merchant outbids a noble house, the house loses. This creates excellent drama.

Q: Can the King rig an auction?

A: Indirectly. The King can fund a preferred bidder secretly, but cannot override the auction result directly. If discovered, this creates scandal. Rigging is possible but risky.

Q: What if no one bids in an auction?

A: Failed auction ‚Üí property reverts to OPEN ‚Üí King may convert to Royal Grant or re-auction later. No one is forced to take unwanted property.

Q: Can noble houses lose their manor permanently?

A: Yes. If evicted and the King auctions it, and the house cannot/does not bid, they become homeless. A noble house without land is weakened but still exists. Homelessness is a valid state.

Q: How do I know what properties are available?

A: Ask a Steward. They maintain public knowledge of OPEN properties, pending auctions, and upcoming grants. Alternatively, a Public Property Board could list this (bulletin board or gump).

Q: Can units share a single property?

A: No. Each property is assigned to exactly one unit or individual role. However, units can have multiple residents/members who share access. Sharing happens inside the unit, not across units.

Q: What happens to items in an auctioned property?

A: Per Model 1 (if it's in the house, it's at risk), eviction gives 72 hours to remove belongings. After that, items left behind transfer with the property or are considered abandoned. Auction winners inherit an empty building unless the previous occupant left things behind.

Final Simplified Rules

Use this verbatim:

Properties are assigned by method, not discretion.
Royal grants are decided by the Crown.
Noble house and guild properties are allocated internally.
Private properties are won by auction.
Individuals and units may hold limited multiple properties, but each additional holding increases exposure to eviction and loss.

Succession Mechanics & Role Transfer

When a role changes hands (death, abdication, election), property ownership must transfer cleanly without manual intervention.

The Transfer Process

Automatic Succession Flow

  1. Trigger Event: Role assignment changes (death, succession, election)
  2. System Action: TransferEstateToRoleHolder(roleId, newCharacter)
  3. House Update:
    • Set house.Owner = newCharacter
    • Clear personal co-owners (clean slate)
    • Restore role-based staff from template
    • Preserve institutional bans
    • Update house sign to show role name
  4. Logging: Record transfer in Land Ledger
  5. Announcement: Public notification (optional)

What Transfers vs. What Persists

Element On Succession Reasoning
House Ownership Transfers to new role holder Keys follow the role, not the person
Personal Co-owners Cleared/wiped Clean succession, no inherited personal friends
Role-based Staff Persists (re-added from template) Guards, stewards are institutional, not personal
Institutional Bans Persist Exiles remain exiled regardless of who holds role
Personal Items Depends on design choice See edge cases below
Vendors Persist if institutional Court vendors remain; personal shops may pack up
Sign Name Shows role, not person "King of Britain's Palace" not "Bob's House"

Critical Edge Cases

Case 1: Role-Holder Dies Inside Estate

Problem: If the King dies in the throne room, and ownership immediately transfers, there's a brief lockout window.

Solution: Interregnum State

  • On death of role-holder: Estate enters INTERREGNUM state
  • During interregnum:
    • Doors remain unlocked OR designated steward NPC can open
    • No lockdowns can be added
    • No evictions can occur
  • On new role assignment: Ownership locks back down
  • Duration: Until successor is assigned (max 72 hours)

Case 2: Personal Items Left Behind

Design Options:

Option A: Role Inventory Persists (Recommended)

All items in role-held estates are institutional property. When King dies, successor inherits everything. Simple, clear, RP-friendly.

Pros: Clean, no edge cases, encourages use of personal storage elsewhere

Cons: Players may feel item loss is harsh

Option B: Personal Container Auto-Pack

System identifies "personal" containers (marked somehow) and auto-packs them to a neutral vault or executor.

Pros: Players keep personal items

Cons: Complex, requires container tagging system

Option C: Grace Period Retrieval

72-hour window where old character can enter to remove items before lockdown.

Pros: Fair warning system

Cons: Requires ghost/spirit access, creates awkward RP

Case 3: Role-Swap Exploitation

Exploit Risk: Player rapidly swaps into role ‚Üí gains house access ‚Üí steals items ‚Üí swaps out

Mitigations:

  • Witness Requirement: Role assumption requires another player in official capacity to witness
  • Location Requirement: Must occur at designated Seat Hall (throne room, court)
  • Cooldown Timer: 10-30 minute cooldown before swapping roles again
  • Log Everything: All role assumptions logged with timestamp and witness

Case 4: Multi-Property Collapse

When a noble house holding 3 properties (manor + 2 auxiliaries) is evicted:

  • Eviction applies to ALL properties simultaneously
  • 72-hour notice applies to all
  • At expiry, all 3 revert to OPEN status
  • Can be re-granted or auctioned separately

Impact: Total collapse is intentionally devastating. Makes eviction a serious political weapon.

Case 5: Auction Winner Already at Max

Scenario: Player bids in auction but already holds max properties

Solution:

  • Auction UI checks property count BEFORE accepting bid
  • If at max: "You must relinquish a property before bidding"
  • Alternative: Allow "conditional bid" ‚Äî player designates which property to forfeit if they win

ServUO Implementation Pseudocode

TransferEstateToRoleHolder() Function

public static void TransferEstateToRoleHolder(Role role, Mobile newHolder)
{
    BaseHouse house = FindHouseByRole(role.RoleId);
    
    if (house == null || newHolder == null)
        return;
    
    // 1) Transfer ownership
    house.Owner = newHolder;
    
    // 2) Clean personal access
    house.CoOwners.Clear();
    house.Friends.Clear();
    
    // 3) Restore institutional staff
    foreach (var staffRole in role.GetStaffRoles())
    {
        Mobile staffMember = staffRole.CurrentHolder;
        if (staffMember != null)
            house.Friends.Add(staffMember);
    }
    
    // 4) Preserve institutional bans (do nothing - they persist)
    
    // 5) Update sign
    if (house.Sign != null)
        house.Sign.SetName($"{role.DisplayName}");
    
    // 6) Log the transfer
    LandLedger.LogTransfer(house, newHolder, role, "Succession");
    
    // 7) Optional: Public announcement
    Announce($"{role.DisplayName} now held by {newHolder.Name}");
}

Integration: Succession + Auctions + Eviction

Complete Lifecycle Example

Scenario: Noble House Rise & Fall

  1. Initial Grant: King grants House Rowan a manor via Royal Grant
    • Steward assigns Manor ‚Üí Rowan House role
    • Rowan Primary gains house ownership
  2. Growth: Rowan wins auction for warehouse (auxiliary property #1)
    • System checks: Rowan has 1 manor, can hold 2 more
    • Auction succeeds, warehouse assigned
  3. Internal Succession: Rowan Primary dies (permadeath)
    • House selects new Primary internally
    • Both properties transfer to new Primary automatically
    • Steward is NOT involved
  4. Crime & Eviction: Rowan harbors exile, King orders eviction
    • 72-hour notice posted on BOTH properties
    • Rowan has time to remove items
    • At expiry: both properties revert to OPEN
  5. Redistribution: King decides:
    • Manor ‚Üí Royal Grant to House Hawkwright (reward loyalty)
    • Warehouse ‚Üí Public auction (commoner opportunity)
  6. Auction Drama: Rowan attempts to bid from exile
    • System blocks: "Exiled individuals cannot bid"
    • Rival merchant wins warehouse instead

Result: Complete property lifecycle without staff intervention. System enforces rules; politics provides drama.

Castle-Specific Succession

For complex buildings like Britain Castle with multiple zones and apartments:

What Changes When King Dies

Element Behavior
Royal Suite access Transfers to new King
Crown administrative rights Transfers to new King
Chancellor's room NO CHANGE (separate role)
Guard barracks NO CHANGE (group-owned)
Noble apartments NO CHANGE (separate roles)
Public zones NO CHANGE (always accessible)
Design Win: When the King dies, only King-specific access changes. The castle doesn't collapse into chaos.

Staff Access Groups (Prevents Re-Adding Everyone)

Define access by role groups, not individual names:

Access Group Members Access Granted
RoyalFamily King, Queen, Heirs Private royal zones, administrative
RoyalGuard Marshal, Guard Captain, Guards Military zones, patrol routes
CastleStaff Stewards, servants, cooks Service areas, kitchens, storage
Council Chancellor, Magistrates, advisors Council chamber, archives
Envoy Temporary diplomatic guests Guest quarters (time-limited)

When staff hired/fired:

  • Add/remove from group membership
  • No manual door edits needed
  • Access automatically updates based on group

Final Mental Model

Properties are not owned by people. They are held by roles. When someone dies, the role remains; only the occupant changes.

This means:

  • Death doesn't break the housing system
  • Succession is automatic and logged
  • Access follows institutional logic, not personal favor
  • Eviction is devastating but clean
  • Auctions create legitimate upward mobility
